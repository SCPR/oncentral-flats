Report: Recess is crucial for kids' mental, social skills

Jan. 4, 2013, 4 p.m.

Many schools cancel recess to make more time for academics but this can have negative effects on student's developments. (Flickr via Neighborhood Centers)


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

Although recess has been proven to have social, mental and physical benefits for kids, a new report says this free time is being reduced in time and scope at many U.S. schools. According to "The Crucial Role of Recess in School" in Pediatrics journal, recess is often limited to make more time for classroom learning -- but with negative results.

"Recess serves as a necessary break from the rigors of concentrated, academic challenges in the classroom," according to the report from the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP). "Recess is unique from, and a complement to, physical education—not a substitute for it."

The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention defines recess as “regularly scheduled periods within the elementary school day for unstructured physical activity and play.” In the U.S., recess can range from 20 to 60 minutes; it can stand alone or be had before or after a specified lunch period. Pediatricians say that when recess is given before lunch, there is often less food waste than if they have lunch and are given a break after.

AAP reports that recess helps kids develop tools they'll use for the rest of their lives: sharing, perseverance, communication and cooperation. This break from the classroom provides students with a designated time to exercise; helping them to maintain a healthy weight and get a break from the structure of the classroom.

According to Pediatrics, the amount of recess times decreases as students age. Recess time is also often shorter in urban areas or communities of lower socioeconomic status.

Scholastic reports that reducing recess time began gaining in popularity after the No Child Left Behind act. Schools were under pressure to show academic progress so they began cutting free time in lieu of academic instruction. According to the Pediatrics report, "minimizing or eliminating recess may be counterproductive to academic achievement, as a growing body of evidence suggests that recess promotes not only physical health and social development but also cognitive performance."

Scholastic adds that in addition to academic concerns, some schools have gotten rid of recess because of safety concerns, lack of supervision, and sub-par playground equipment. Instead of canceling recess, pediatricians recommend establishing and enforcing safety rules, providing adequate adult supervision, and regularly inspecting playground equipment.
