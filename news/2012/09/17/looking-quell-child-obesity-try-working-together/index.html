Looking to quell child obesity? Try working together

Sept. 17, 2012, 3:57 p.m.

South Los Angeles Wetland Park on the day of its grand opening in February 2012. Councilwoman Jan Perry pointed to the park as an important anti-obesity initiative, since it provides a place for southside residents to exercise. (José Martinez/OnCentral)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

South L.A. is seriously struggling with child obesity, but a new study suggests that could change with some focused community action.

Of course, that's easier said than done, and the problem is particularly pronounced on the southside, with approximately 30 percent of its youth qualifying as obese.

But researchers in Providence, R.I. were working with the same kind of population when they decided to try implementing a six-month-long obesity intervention program – except rather than have clinics carry them out, they made it so community members were the ones making it happen.

Here's what they did: Parents and guardians received a curriculum that focused on avoiding junk food, exercising and dealing with peer pressure. They then led 24 sessions – 12 with their kids at home and 12 in a large group setting with the rest of the program's participants – in which they walked kids through the different points of each "lesson."

At the program's commencement, 92 percent of the participating kids were obese, and half were at or above the 99th percentile – "off the charts," as researchers put it.

By the time it was over, 8 percent fewer kids were at or above the 99th percentile, and there were 10 percent fewer obese kids.

"If a program like this were implemented across the country, there would be 10 percent fewer obese children," said Gary Foster, who developed the program, in a statement. "That's a huge impact with significant health and economic implications."

L.A. City Councilwoman Jan Perry, who oversees the city's ninth council district, says South Los Angeles is one community among many nationwide that need jobs, food choices and exercise options. Foster's program is "absolutely" something that could work in her district, she said.

"There are a number of places it could be applied," said Perry, mentioning schools and area health care organizations.

With obesity, said Perry, comes heart disease, diabetes and respiratory illnesses. "Obesity is the one issue we can attack as a community health issue to drive [those diseases'] numbers down and improve them," she said.

Her efforts have been legislative, she said, pointing to the two grocery stores she brought into the area, as well as parks she both restored and built. She highlighted South Los Angeles Wetland Park, located near the intersection of 54th Street and Avalon Boulevard, in particular.

"There are adults over there constantly exercising in groups, so clearly people want to be able to do something to work on obesity and stay healthy," said Perry.

The councilwoman said providing healthy food options is crucial to beating back obesity in the area.

"What would make that extra push [is] when other grocery stores realize that they can make money in this market because people are very enthusiastic about healthy eating when it is put before them," she said. "They embrace it and they spend their dollars locally."

For a program like Foster's to work, then, it wouldn't take much besides a bit of organization and infrastructure. As Perry says, "Demand is limitless at this point."

Foster admitted that his community-based program isn't as effective as medical intervention would be.

"But it is more cost-efficient and more accessible to the millions of families who need help," he said.

Sounds like the southside.
