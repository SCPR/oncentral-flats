Historic YMCA transformed into $21 million housing complex

Dec. 5, 2012, 2:08 p.m.

The historic YMCA has maintained its architectural features while being renovated for livability.


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

The historic 28th Street YMCA in South L.A. reopened this week after a $21 million makeover transformed the building into 29 brand new apartments for low-income adults and young people who are transitioning out of homelessness. The renovation, co-developed by Clifford Beers Housing (CBH) and Coalition for Responsible Community Development (CRCD), includes redoing the original 24 YMCA units and creating an entirely new wing of 25 studio apartments.

The YMCA's indoor basketball court was standing-room-only on the day of the opening, as area Councilwoman Jan Perry tearfully thanked everyone who helped the renovation come to fruition. A statement from Perry's office described the YMCA as not just a decades-old building, but a key part of South L.A.'s history.

The structure was designed by "renown black architect, Paul Williams, known for designing luxury homes for the rich and famous." Williams was also the first black member of the American Institute of Architects and when the original YMCA building was completed in 1926, it was exclusively for black men.

"It [the building] had good bones because he was a good architect," said Perry the day of the grand opening.

A statement from the councilwoman also said that the building "represents both noteworthy architecture and an especially poignant artifact of the era of racial segregation." Now in a very different era, the renovated building has almost 50 units with their own private bathrooms and kitchenettes, along with shared communal space, a garden and a new bay of washer and dryers.

According to Perry's office, 35 percent of the housing units are set aside for people with disabilities and many of these residents will have mental illnesses. The Kedren Community Health Center is partnering with the new YMCA to provide the necessary mental health help.

“We can't over-emphasize the importance and need for affordable supportive housing for the community," said Kedren's president and CEO, Dr. John Griffith, in a statement. “The services provided to the residents will make the important difference in helping to transform lives of the persons in recovery.”

Griffith added that Kedren's approach to mental well-being is shifting from treating illness to promoting wellness. On the Kedren website he describes their approach to mental health as "integrating programs that focus on early intervention and prevention, serving children from birth to older adults, with a strengths-based, family-focused model to enrich the lives of those we serve, planting the seeds of physical, social, emotional and cognitive growth, and overall wellness."

According to a 2011 report from the National Survey on Drug Use and Health, one in five U.S. adults had a mental illness last year but only 40 percent received treatment. Elena Fernandez, the director of behavioral health at St. John's Well Child and Family Center, told OnCentral that "especially in impoverished communities like South L.A.," resources for mental health care are scarce.

In addition to mental health help, the new 28th Street housing complex will include social and medical services, as well as educational and job training opportunities for youth. Because the building is on the City of Los Angeles Registry of Historic Resources, improvements to the structure are meant to "respect the existing historic facades and architectural features" while updating amenities and design elements to make the space comfortable and contemporary.

The redesign was led by Koning Eizenberg Architecture and built to LEED Gold criteria -- meaning the building was also designed to be "green" and energy-efficient.
