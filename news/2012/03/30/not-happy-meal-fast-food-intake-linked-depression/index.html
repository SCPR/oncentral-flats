Not a Happy Meal: Fast food intake linked to depression

March 30, 2012, 3:49 p.m.

A new Spanish study has found that people who eat a lot of fast food are 51 percent more likely to become depressed. (Credit: Calgary Reviews/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

That Happy Meal might be putting you at risk to become a whole lot sadder, according to Spanish researchers.

In a study of 12,059 Spanish university graduates who had an average age of about 37-and-a-half years old, researchers found that depression is 51 percent more likely to occur in people who consume large amounts of fast food than people who don't.

The study took place between 1999 and 2010.

South L.A. is no stranger to fast food. In fact, fast-food establishments make up nearly 72 percent of the eateries on the southside, which was part of the reason Ninth District Councilwoman Jan Perry concentrated efforts to bring more healthy food options into her district.

"I prefer to focus on trying to create options, bringing more restaurants, bringing in more grocery stores," she told OnCentral earlier in March.

The prevalence of fast food in the region has been linked to obesity, diabetes, heart disease, and now depression. In 2011, the L.A. County Department of Public Health released figures that showed that 13.6 percent of adults in South L.A. had been diagnosed with depressive disorder at least once by 2007.

The report also said the risk of depression is higher the more co-occurring chronic conditions a person has – conditions like diabetes, high cholesterol, heart disease and obesity, all of which can be exacerbated or even caused by excessive fast-food intake, and all of which are particular problems for South Los Angeles.

Almudena Sanchez-Villegas, the study's lead author, said in a statement that the "more fast food you consume, the greater the risk of depression."

But according to ABC News, other experts are suggesting it's only an association, not causal. In other words: It's not the food that's causing the depression.

"High intake of fast food may very well increase risks of depression by causing poor health in general," David Katz, the director of Yale University's Prevention Research Center told ABC. "But depression may also increase fast-food intake."

That's where the term "comfort food" comes from, he added.

"[Comfort food] can help alleviate stress, anxiety and depression," Katz explained. "So it may be that people with depression are turning to [fast food] for relief."

If that's the case, there won't be any trouble with access on the southside for people who are already depressed. Keith Ayoob, a dietician and professor at Albert Einstein College of Medicine in New York, said to "keep it in context."

"It does not mean that if you go and eat a hamburger you are going to become depressed," he said to ABC. "I think this represents a refection of depression, not the cause.
