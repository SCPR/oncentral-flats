UMMA founding president and CEO will step down after 13 years

June 7, 2012, 4:32 p.m.

UMMA Community Clinic founding president and CEO Yasser Aman at the groundbreaking for Fremont High School Community Clinic in April. Aman will step down from his position after 13 years with UMMA in December. (Credit: José Martinez/OnCentral)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

For 13 years, Yasser Aman has led UMMA Community Health Clinic in its effort to improve health in overwhelmingly unhealthy South Los Angeles.

UMMA – the University Muslim Medical Association – announced on Wednesday that, effective December 1, Aman will step down.

"UMMA's founders could not have known how lucky they were, 16 years ago, on the day when Yasser signed up as a volunteer," said Murtaza Sanwari, chair of the board, in a statement. "I speak for all the directors when I say that we could not be more grateful to him."

Aman is leaving voluntarily, and will remain on UMMA's advisory council.

"I think one truly has to reflect on their effectiveness and impact, and you don't necessarily want to weigh down any organization if you feel as if you're not as effective as possible," Aman said. "It was the result of a lot of introspection and conversations. There's never a good or right time and I think I just had to make that decision."

Aman was in school for 10 of his 13 years as UMMA's first CEO – he got his Masters and doctorate in public health – and after graduating last year, he said it was time to start "rethinking and re-prioritizing," in particular because he wants to spend more time with his family.

He came on board with UMMA as a volunteer in 1995, while he was still a student a UCLA. He organized the clinic's volunteer schedule before it officially opened in 1996, when it operated two days a week and when its staff was comprised entirely of volunteers.

Four years later, Aman was promoted to CEO. Since then, UMMA became federally-qualified, grew its staff to 35 paid employees and extended its hours to six days a week. The clinic serves thousands that wouldn't be able to get medical care otherwise – about 70 percent are uninsured, and only one percent pay in full for their services.

"By far, UMMA has given me personally a lot more than I have ever given the organization," said Aman. "I always say that the word 'university' in our name really is symbolic for me because I really call myself a student of the UMMA Clinic. It's taught me so much about humanity and the capacity of people with significant differences in background to do what is right and to do what is important for the community."

For the interim, UMMA has appointed a transition committee to oversee the search for both an interim and permanent CEO. The clinic is looking to hire an interim CEO by October 1 and a permanent CEO by the first day of 2013, in time to oversee the opening of UMMA's new school-based health clinic. at Fremont High School.

As for what's next, Aman doesn't really know. "I'm looking for a job," he laughed.

But he takes his UMMA experience with him.

"This journey has just really been an amazing learning opportunity, about myself," he said. "But also just to be in awe of all the amazing people that have touched or experienced this clinic, either through receiving service or supporting it from afar."
