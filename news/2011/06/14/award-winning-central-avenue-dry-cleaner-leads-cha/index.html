Vivian Bowers: Award-winning Central Avenue businesswoman

June 14, 2011, 12:58 p.m.

Vivian Bowers is the president of the Central Avenue Business Association and owner of Bower and Sons Cleaners.


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

The sign reads Bowers and Sons Cleaners, but it is now Bowers’ daughter Vivian who is running the family business, patrolling the streets of Central Avenue and serving as a role model for surrounding new and long-standing businesses.

Bowers wants her dry-cleaning business to feel like home to her customers and it seems to be working — as mothers, police officers, elderly men and others cycle in and out of the store to drop off or pick up clothes, and Bowers calls them each by name.

The entryway of the store is carefully decorated by Bowers’ husband, and features an orchestrated collage of mounted newspaper clippings, dry cleaning awards and framed record covers from music legends like Smokey Robinson.

Bowers and Sons has weathered repeated riots, devastating earthquakes and the severe recent economic recession. Three generations of family members and 61 years of service later, Vivian Bowers received the 46th Assembly District Small Business Leader of the Year award earlier this month.

“To finally be recognized for the struggles, it’s not just me, it’s my family,” Bowers said.

Bowers took over the family business in 1994, leaving her job as an account representative at Kaiser Permanente and forfeiting the health insurance, paid vacations and a 401(k) that went with it.

“Income is good, and it was very sweet, but being happy when you go to work…it makes all the difference in the world,” Bowers said.

On a stretch of South Los Angeles that hosts Mexican bakeries, liquor stores and shuttered, empty lots, Bowers familial, giraffe-themed dry cleaning business stands out. Bowers works seven days a week, often 14 hours a day, running the business as well smaller tasks, like cleaning clothes, doing alterations, working the counter and greeting customers.

“Today, in our business climate, I am an employee again," Bowers said. "I always will be to a certain level, but I am now in the trenches.”

Bowers is also the president of the Central Avenue Business Association (CABA), which works to revitalize and revamp the business landscape of Central Avenue. Bowers is genuine and whole-hearted in her commitment to the livelihood of Central Avenue, so much so that she purchased a second location for her dry cleaning business to avoid seeing another shuttered storefront.

“The man was going to close his store and I couldn’t see yet another vacant building,” Bowers said. “There’s so much blithe along Central Avenue, you can’t add to it.”

Bowers encouraging businesses to join CABA and think creatively with their business plans, leading by example. A few years ago, Bowers had her building repainted and new signs and awnings installed. By the time the cosmetic overhaul was complete, multiple surrounding businesses had filed paperwork with the city to tap into redevelopment funds to do the same.

Bowers recalls Central Avenue’s “suited and booted” heyday in the bustling 1940’s, when law firms, banks, theaters and a bustling nightlife was burgeoning out of this South L.A. community. Now, Central Avenue doesn’t even have a single sit-down restaurant, Bowers said.

But progress can be a challenge for businesses struggling just to survive. Local business owners Terry and Sparky Ferguson have been forced to renovate and reinvent their business, Doug’s Furniture, multiple times since its establishment in 1938.

“Every year the store got smaller. Every riot,” Sparky Ferguson said.

Doug’s was destroyed in both the 1960s and ‘90s riots, eventually causing the family to get rid of all the windows in the store as a safety precaution. The recent economic downturn and flagging business has spurred the Ferguson’s to start selling janitorial supplies in their furniture store, and reinstall two large, front windows in an attempt to attract more business.

Bowers encourages all her neighbors to take pride in their businesses, adapt to trying times and think about improving the community as a whole.

Bowers tries to promote better business standards, like regularly cleaning storefront windows and treating customers with respect — never taking for granted that they will always come back. She emphasizes the need for embracing and instigating change on Central Avenue and the businesses that dot its streets.

"I can see little sparks. I can see them say, ‘Yes, if she can do it, I can do it’," Bowers said.
