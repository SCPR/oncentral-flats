El Santo Nino Community Center remains dedicated to providing a safe haven

July 8, 2011, 1:19 p.m.

Children walk by the community center


By Dion Rabouin
Comment

Tweet

Earlier this year, Fernando Sarabia was awarded the Bien Hecho Award for dedication and commitment to the Hispanic community. Despite being one of only eight people in the nation to receive the honor, Sarabia doesn’t talk much about it. Instead, he shifts the conversation to the El Santo Nino Community Center and the people it serves.

He’s El Santo Nino’s group worker and has been there for more than eight years, almost half as a volunteer. In a neighborhood with gang violence, illiteracy and poverty, El Santo Nino has been providing a safe place for children, teens and families for decades.

“The center provides subsistence to everybody in the community,” Sarabia said. “My mission is to get these kids away from the streets and to achieve a good education and go to college. A lot of these kids don’t even finish high school, so we’re trying to avoid that and help them as much as we can.”

The center is located at the corner of 23rd and Trinity St. and is open seven days a week. It features services like computer training, ESL classes, after-school tutoring and licensed child care. There’s also a mass held in Spanish every Sunday. When available, the center also provides clothes, food vouchers and bus tokens.

The Bien Hecho Award brought with it a donation of $5,000. That may have been the biggest victory. While there are issues with gangs, drugs and violence in the area, the center’s site coordinator Veona Gibson said her biggest worry was making sure the center was still able to serve the community.

“We are always trying to find funds for this community center,” Gibson said. “We don’t want people in this community to fall between the cracks. We need to keep these kids out of gangs and to do that we need programs.”

Arcelia Flores, who brings her 6-year-old daughter Jazmin to the center, is glad El Santo Nino is around because it provides help that sometimes parents are unable to provide.

“I like that it’s a place that [my daughter] can do homework, because I don’t speak English and can’t help her,” Flores said through a translator. “I don’t have to worry about her because she is safe here.”

Until last year, El Santo Nino, which is a part of Catholic Charities of Los Angeles, also had a school readiness program and a preschool for children aged three to five. Those were lost when the city of Los Angeles cut funding for the program.

“We need the school readiness program. We need the afterschool program,” said Gibson. “You have to start when the kid is small. You don’t grab a teenager and tell him, ‘Let me teach you how to read.’”

Even in the face of such circumstances, El Santo Nino has plenty of success stories. Sarabia said one notable alumnus, Genesis Artiaga, started school at Princeton University last year. He and Gibson are most proud when former students like her come back and volunteer at the center.

“When people come back that’s when we know that we did something good for that person,” Sarabia said. “That means that we’re doing a good job.”
