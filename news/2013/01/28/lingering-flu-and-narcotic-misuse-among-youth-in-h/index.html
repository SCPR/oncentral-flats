Lingering flu and narcotic misuse among youth: In health news today

Jan. 28, 2013, 8:54 a.m.

Most parents aren't concerned about young people's misusing narcotics, says a new poll. (Paula Bailey/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

Flu isn't quite done with the U.S. yet, reports ABCNews.com. While some areas are seeing the virus in decline, the southwest and northwest regions of the country are seeing their flu cases rise. Last week, 9.8 percent of deaths were attributed to the flu and pneumonia, up from 8.3 percent the week before.

More obesity in children means more cases of type 2 diabetes, and that means a new first for the medical community: TIME reports that the American Academy of Pediatrics has released the first-ever guidelines for treating diabetes in young people between 10 and 18.

Three more drugs have been approved to treat type 2 diabetes, all of them containing something called alogliptin. HealthDay quotes an official from the Food and Drug Administration who says the substance "helps stimulate the release of insulin after a meal, which leads to better blood sugar control." The three new drugs are Nesina, Kazano and Oseni.

The official goal of health officials in the U.S. is to get smoking prevalence down to 12 percent by 2020, reports the Los Angeles Times. Right now, it's at 21.2 percent – and researchers from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) say if current trends continue unabated, that goal simply won't be met.

The CDC also reported on Friday that bisexual women are more likely to suffer from domestic violence than their heterosexual or lesbian counterparts. HealthDay noted the report was the first to look the relationship between sexual orientation and rates of intimate partner violence, sexual violence and stalking.

USA Today says that most U.S. adults – about 70 percent – say they're tracking their health issues: diet, weight, exercise routines, medical symptoms. But what that usually means, says new research, is that they're just keeping track in their heads. In other words, most people aren't using technology to track their health, although researchers say such technologies will soon be mainstream.

And finally: HealthDay has news on a poll that asked parents how concerned they were about young people's misuse of narcotics and pain medications. Despite the fact that abuse and overdose trends are rising in all age groups, parents generally said they weren't all that concerned – which means they probably aren't all that aware, said pollsters.
