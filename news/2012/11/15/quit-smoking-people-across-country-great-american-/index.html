Quit smoking with people across the country for the Great American Smokeout

Nov. 15, 2012, 12:36 p.m.

The Great Smokeout is today and aims to help smokers stop smoking. (Flickr via SuperFantastic)


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

Although smoking cigarettes or being exposed to second-hand smoke results in more than 443,000 deaths a year, there are still more than 43 million people in the U.S. who smoke -- so today on the annual Great American Smokeout, health officials are encouraging these smokers to quit.

The American Cancer Society sponsors the event and says that whether it's quitting for just one day, making a plan to quit in the future or committing to a lifetime of being tobacco-free, people should use the Smokeout as a jumping off point for a healthier life.

This countrywide stop-smoking campaign takes place every year on the third Thursday of November. The Cancer Society says nearly 70 percent of smokers in the U.S. say they would like to quit and 52 percent report making at least one serious attempt to do so each each year -- but only 4 percent are actually successful in quitting.

Since the Smokeout began 37 years ago, people have learned a great deal more about not only the effects of smoking but why it has such an addictive hold on so many people, writes Thomas J. Glynn, PhD on the Cancer Society's Expert Blog.

"For instance, we now know that nicotine is a highly addictive drug - nearly as powerful as heroin and cocaine - and that smoking is both physically addictive (due to the pull of nicotine) and psychologically addictive (due to the rituals we build around smoking), making quitting a challenge for most smokers," Glynn said.

Tobacco use remains the largest preventable cause of disease and premature death in the US, widely recognized for leading to multiple forms of cancer including lung and mouth. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), smoking costs the U.S. about $96 billion each year in medical expenses and $97 billion in lost productivity due to premature death.

While if often takes smokers multiple times to quit for good, you may begin seeing improvements in your health less than half an hour after putting down your pipe or cigarette. The CDC says that 20 minutes after you quit smoking your heart rate drops; 12 hours after the carbon monoxide level in your blood drops to normal; and only three weeks after quitting the physical symptoms of nicotine addiction end. By about five years after quitting, the CDC says your chance for cancer of the mouth, throat, esophagus, and bladder is cut in half.

In South Los Angeles, close to 20 percent of the population smokes according to the latest numbers from the L.A. County Department of Public Health: This is compared to the 13 percent of residents over the age of 18 who smoke across the entire county.

When breaking these numbers down by race and ethnicity, blacks are the most likely demographic to smoke, with 17 percent of black residents in L.A. being smokers. Whites are the next largest group of smokers, with more than 15 percent of white people smoking across the county as well.

To find out if you need help quitting you can take the Cancer Society quiz, or for more information on how to quit once you've decided to, check out their extensive guidelines and tip sheet.
