How to have a DIY holiday; saving you money and the planet

Nov. 16, 2012, 2:11 p.m.

One way to save money and cut down on holiday waste is to wrap presents with recycled paper or misc. items you already have. (Flickr via allerleirau)


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

It's nearly Thanksgiving which means the holiday season is officially upon us. In a time full of excess -- excess spending, excess shopping, excess eating -- there's a lot of ways to save on money and "go green" in the process.

According to the National Retail Federation (NRF), the average holiday shopper this year will spend $749.51 on gifts, cards, decorations and other holiday-related goods. Six in 10 shoppers will spend a portion of this money on themselves -- "self-gifting" while they shop for friends and family. To reduce expenses and cut gas emissions, the Centers for Disease and Control and Prevention (CDC) suggests taking public transit or carpooling to do your shopping. Better yet, stay home and shop online; you can have the gifts sent directly to you or the recipient and will be able to quickly find the best deal.

If you do head out to do in-person shopping, the CDC recommends buying gifts from antique or second-hand stores, or going to a craft fair where you can purchase goods from local businesses. If you opt out of the in-store gift wrapping and wrap the presents yourself, you can cut down on waste and money, and put your own personal touch on the gift.

HGTV reports that the U.S. produces an extra three million tons of waste over the holiday season, most of which is cards and wrapping paper. To cut down on these numbers, they suggest recycling old fabric, scarves or even using flower pots to creatively wrap holiday gifts. You can also try purchasing greeting cards made from recycled paper or making a homemade card; this option can save you a lot of money over the season as store-bought cards can range from 99 cents to a few dollars.

You can even cut down on costs and waste when you prepare your Thanksgiving feast or other holiday meals. The CDC recommends buying in bulk to avoid excess packaging and encourages people to break out the non-disposable utensils -- not only does it look nice, it cuts down on trash.

Other tips for a happy and "green" holiday:

- Buy LED holiday lights that according to the Sierra Club, use 90 percent less energy than normal holiday lights
- Although it might not smell the same as a real Christmas tree, if you buy an artificial tree you can reuse it year after year -- and there will be a lot less mess
- Take your own reusable totes or bags when you go shopping to create less waste
- Buy gifts that are tickets to events, a membership to a museum, or other presents that include minimal packaging and wrapping
