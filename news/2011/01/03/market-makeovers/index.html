Investing in vegetables with Market Makeovers

Jan. 3, 2011, 5:05 p.m.

Coronado Meat Market was the site where the first Market Makeover in South Los Angeles took place.


By Doris Quintanilla
Comment

Tweet

When his goddaughter first approached him in regards to giving his market, Coronado Meat Market, a makeover, Jose Coronado thought, "Why not?"

Originally a school/community project in partnership between several community organizations and local schools, Market Makeovers aimed to make over local community markets in the South Los Angeles in order to showcase more healthy food options. The change stuck, and three years later, Jose Coronado says business has never been better.

Jose Coronado, owner and manager of Coronado Market on 42nd Street and Avalon, says that his 16-year-old business was the first store to participate in the Market Makeover project. Coronado said the market is last one standing of those that took part in the program. Why?

"Small markets are scared to invest in vegetables,” Coronado said. "Fifty percent of it goes to waste and gets thrown out."

But the change in his store did not waver customers, nor his investment in fresh produce. There have been more people, he said, and customers are happy with the change.

The concept of the makeover is simple enough. Market owners rearrange the location of their products within the store, in a way to have produce at the front, in hopes of grabbing their customers' attention and have them eat healthier.

As part of the change, Coronado moved the Pepsi cooler that was once in front of the store towards the back. A simple remodeling of the store, but the Pepsi Company had something to say about it.

“They didn't like it but I told them they had two options,” Coronado said. “They could either accept it or take their business elsewhere. Of course, they accepted it.”

Mike Blockstein, president of Public Matters, an organization that worked on Market Makeovers, said he was not surprised when he heard of the situation.

"A company like Pepsi comes in and they give the local small businesses free stuff, and all they ask in return is for the markets to distribute their products, and by putting their products at the back of the store, you are moving them out of the main light of sight," Blockstein said. "These companies are very good and efficient when it comes to marketing their products."

Coronado believes those companies will do fine with not having their products in the front of the store. “They have commercials, kids come in to buy their products, but it's harder to show a commercial for an apple.”

Market Makeover started out as a research project, in partnership with East Los Angeles College, UCLA, and USC and the South Los Angeles Healthy Eating Active Community. It aimed to increase vegetables and produce in local markets. The program is no longer active in South Los Angeles, and has moved on to East Los Angeles. But Blockstein hopes it left an impact on the stores it made over, the customers, and the people that were involved, particularly the youth.

"The high school students were ambassadors, spokespeople, models, media, teach creatively, and educate people," Blockstein said. "The youth are visible advocates who rallied around things that they were personally affected by, they took it home and tried to change behaviors, both theirs and those around them."

As for the food business owners that are interested in giving their establishment a market makeover, Gwendolyn Flynn, the Community Health Policy and Education Director for Community Health Councils, Inc., suggested that they "partner up with local community groups and individuals like parents in schools, and churches," that are also working into bringing healthy eating into their neighborhoods.

"I hear businesses that are scared to invest in produce," Flynn added. "But at the same time I hear of consumers who leave their communities to get fresh food." Communication between the two groups, marketing and promotion, said Flynn, are key to making healthy food access successful.
