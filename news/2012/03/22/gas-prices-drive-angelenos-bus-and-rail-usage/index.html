Gas prices drive Angelenos to bus and rail usage

March 22, 2012, 1:55 p.m.

Long-distance bus lines and rail usage is up in Los Angeles, according to Metro. (Credit: Brian De Los Santos/OnCentral)


By Brian De Los Santos
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

The amount of people taking public transportation is up in Los Angeles, according to Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (Metro), but as for the South Los Angeles region, it may be business as usual.

Marc Littman, Metro’s communication director, said that rail and long-distance bus lines, including the Red, Green, Gold and Blue lines, have seen an increase in passengers in the past couple of months (January and February), but data for bus lines in South L.A. is not included.

Generally, buses don’t just stay in South L.A., he said, since they travel through different communities, making it difficult to track numbers for the area. However, he believes that numbers in South L.A. wouldn’t see a spike in bus or rail usage since many South L.A. locals are public transit-dependent.

“A transit-dependent person is a person who is using [public] transit for his or her everyday commute, not [just out of] necessity but also because of choice,” said Mintesnot Woldeamanuel, professor of urban studies and planning at California State University Northridge.

Although there are cases were people depend on bus or rail because of their socioeconomic background, he said, many times it is a matter of demand and supply. “It depends on the service, not just about low-income,” he said.

Raul Posas, 29, takes the bus to work every weekday, and has been doing so for the last 10 years. He said he takes public transportation because it’s cheaper and the commute to and from work is a short distance.

Sometimes the wait for the bus can be annoying, he said, but that’s only on weekends. He is saving money on insurance and gas. "It's just better," Posas said in Spanish.

Littman said the spike in public transportation usage in L.A. can be attributed to increasing gas prices and an unstable economy. According to LosAngelesGasPrices.com, the average price for gas in Los Angeles is a little over $4, while the national average is less than $4.

“Commuters are really feeling the impact of gas prices,” Littman said. “It affects everybody. You’re going to see the uprise on the rail and long-distance bus.

"It’s far cheaper to use public transit than to drive," he added.

Woldeamanuel said he agreed that gas prices will drive commuters to bus and rail stations, since the burden of high costs will make people think twice about their gas and car budget.
