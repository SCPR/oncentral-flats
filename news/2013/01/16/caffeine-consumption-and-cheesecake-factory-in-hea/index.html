Caffeine consumption and Cheesecake Factory: In health news today

Jan. 16, 2013, 9:15 a.m.

One Cheesecake Factory entrée has 3,120 calories and 89 grams of saturated fat. (Simon Greig/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

Health officials agree: If you're unlucky enough to get the flu, you'll know it's the flu. It's hard to confuse the virus with a common cold, but KPCC has a guide that helps you distinguish between the two anyway.

Some parents are concerned that children receive too many vaccines too soon – so the Institute of Medicine, which advises the government on health, looked into it. According to the Institute's final report, says USA Today, the current standard vaccine schedule is safe for kids, and protects them again "devastating diseases."

Two pounds – that's about the amount of weight a person can lose if she or he cuts down on added sugars in their diets, says a new study. The Los Angeles Times added that increasing the amount of added sugars consumed led to an average weight gain of 1.5 pounds.

There's a risk that gonorrhea will eventually become fully resistant to the antibiotics that are currently used to treat it, reports KPCC, but one local public health expert says L.A. County has nothing to worry about – yet.

U.S. Health and Human Services Secretary Kathleen Sebelius announced Monday that she would allow more time for any states who are interested in creating their own health insurance exchange but missed the previous deadline of Jan. 1, when they were supposed to notify the federal government of their plans. According to HealthDay, as of late December, 18 states and Washington, D.C. declared their intention to run their own exchanges.

About 90 percent of Americans consume caffeine every day, and more than half of them drink more than 300 milligrams daily. Up to 30 percent take in more than 600 milligrams, adds the Chicago Tribune – and that's where the problem lies. An analysis shows that 200 to 300 milligrams of caffeine per day is fine for adults, but more than 500 milligrams a day becomes problematic: It can cause restlessness, sleep disturbance, irritability or headaches (among other things) in some folks.

Soot, also known as black carbon, is the number-two contributor to global warming, says a new study in the Journal of Geophysical Research-Atmospheres, and has double the negative effect on the climate than researchers previously thought.

In what could be a blow for champions of generic drugs over brand-name pharmaceuticals, U.S. researchers claim that the cheaper, generic drugs intended to treat HIV may not be as effective as their branded counterparts. The BBC adds that many are responding by noting that firm conclusions can't be drawn from their research, citing too many unknowns.

In scary health headlines, ABCNews.com reports that at least 11 workers from U.S. biolabs were infected with dangerous pathogens between 2004 and 2010, including the bacteria that cause brucellosis, tularemia and Valley fever fungus. There weren't any confirmed cases of infections transmitted from person to person.

Trying to get teens to be healthy? Text them. HealthDay reports on a study that says sending healthy lifestyle messages via text could help improve teenagers' diets and fitness habits.

And finally: The Baltimore Sun reports on the Center for Science in the Public interest's annual Xtreme Eating Awards, which "honor" meals that the Center's director said seem "scientifically engineered…with the express purpose of promoting obesity, diabetes, and heart disease." Among the winners? Cheesecake Factory's bistro shrimp pasta, with 3,120 calories and 89 grams of saturated fat.
