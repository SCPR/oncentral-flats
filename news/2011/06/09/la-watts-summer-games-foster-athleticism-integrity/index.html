LA Watts Summer Games foster athleticism, integrity and sports legacies

June 9, 2011, 2:33 p.m.

The L.A. Watts Summer Games were founded in 1968 in response to the lasting effects of the Watts riots (photo credit: Erin Powers and the LAJCC)


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

The L.A. Watts Summer Games makes a triumphant return to Los Angeles this weekend, rebounding from their cancellation last year to continue a 43-year legacy of competition and sportsmanship in Los Angeles.

The Summer Games is a three-day athletic tournament that brings together over 5,000 students from more than 200 California high schools. The event began in 1968 as a Junior Olympics created by the Los Angeles Junior Chamber of Commerce (LAJCC).

The Watts Games was the community’s rallied response to the Watts riots that occurred three years prior, said Delece James, board member of the L.A. Junior Chamber of Commerce and this year’s Chairperson of the games.

“They wanted to bring some positive energy back into the community so they decided to start with the youth,” James said.

Team sports unite people across cultural lines and is one of the best ways to learn life lessons like communication, discipline and determination, said Ed Waters, coach of Crenshaw High School’s varsity boys’ basketball team. Waters has been coaching at Crenshaw since 2007 and led the team at Jefferson High School prior to that. Waters’ players know that competing in the Watts Summer Games is a privilege reserved for varsity players.

“I want the lower teams to be hungry in terms of wanting to get to that level,” Waters said.

The Summer Games has produced a legacy of competitive, prosperous athletes, including WNBA sensation Lisa Leslie and hometown hero Marques Johnson. Johnson played high school basketball at Crenshaw, went on to compete in college at UCLA and eventually played on multiple pro teams including the L.A. Clippers and the Golden State Warriors.

While these success stories set inspiring examples for many burgeoning young athletes, the Summer Games serve a broader purpose as well, to remind the younger set of why the games were founded in the first place.

“There are new generations of athletes who aren’t always connected to history,” Waters said.

The Games begin Saturday and will take place at venues across South Los Angeles, including high schools such as Locke, Morningside and Dorsey. The tournament will be mostly double-elimination style and students will compete in basketball, soccer, football, volleyball, softball and lacrosse.

Although the event is all about sports, LAJCC aims to communicate a much larger message.

“They need to understand that although sports are their passion, 90 percent of them probably wont go pro,” James said. “So they need to have a plan or at least a vision of what their life could be after they can no longer play ball. And still be passionate about that life.”
