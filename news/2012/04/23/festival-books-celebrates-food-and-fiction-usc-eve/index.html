Festival of Books celebrates food and fiction with USC event

April 23, 2012, 3:55 p.m.

(credit: Hayley Fox/oncentral) The Festival of Books brought more than 100,000 people to USC last weekend.


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

The Los Angeles Times Festival of Books attracted more than 100,000 people to the University of Southern California this weekend for two days of books sales, author signings and panel discussions.

The campus was bustling but not overcrowded, and on Sunday the childrens' area surrounding the Target stage seemed to be the most popular stop on the expansive campus. A Scooby-Doo booth (complete with Mystery Machine van) nearly caused a traffic jam with its popularity, and people swarmed around a tent selling baby onesies and toddler tees with slogans on them such as "future writer" or screenprints of classic novel covers like "Charlotte's Web."

Panels ranged in topics from fiction and history to D.I.Y tactics and comedy writing. One lively discussion titled "Food Writing: American Potluck" was moderated by Pulitzer-Prize winning food critic Jonathan Gold and included food writers Gustavo Arellano, Aaron Bobrow-Strain and Jennifer 8. Lee.

The three debated the idea of "authentic" ethnic foods in the U.S. and covered everything from the evolution of white bread to a universal disappointment in Tex-Mex cooking. They discussed the variations in "ethnic" cooking from city to city and state to state, and talked about the assimilation of foods to an American palate.

Arellano is now the editor-in-chief of the OC Weekly, and his newest book titled "Taco USA: How Mexican Food Conquered America," covers the reinterpretation of "authentic" Mexican food in different states across the country. In the discussion Sunday, Arellano talked about the California burrito (a burrito stuffed with french fries that was made popular in San Diego) and the proliferation of Mexican restaurants with a name that end in "-berto" (Umberto's, Roberto's, etc).

Bobrow-Strain, author of "White Bread: A Social History of the Store-Bought Loaf," chronicled the societal shift from making bread at home to buying it in stores, and delved into what certain types of bread represent throughout America's history.

Lee, also a published food writer, focused on how "ethnic food" varies in interpretations among different countries and spoke specifically about the culture of Chinese restaurant workers in America. She also explained how dishes like Beef and Broccoli as well as fortune cookies were never actually "Chinese" cuisine.

All three spoke about the changing definition of "authentic" cuisine and how "ethnic" food is reinterpreted in different parts of the world.

In addition to food panels and culinary demonstrations, the festival covered everything from comic books to L.A. history, children's books and Noir. This was the second year the festival was held at USC. Prior to that, it was always across town at UCLA.
