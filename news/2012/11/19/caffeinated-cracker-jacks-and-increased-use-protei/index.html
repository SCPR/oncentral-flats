Caffeinated Cracker Jacks and teen use of protein shakes: In health news today

Nov. 19, 2012, 10:30 a.m.

More teens may be using protein supplements to help bulk. (Flickr via mrcd@sbcglobal.net)


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

A new survey out of the University of Minnesota in Minneapolis shows that teens may be taking more muscle-enhancing supplements than previously expected. ABC reports that although previous studies estimated only 8 percent of females and about 10 percent of males used protein powders and shakes, the new report from Minnesota shows nearly 35 percent of the total population (males and females) uses protein powders and shakes -- while 6 percent said they used steroids.

From the makers of the caramel popcorn and peanut snack Cracker Jacks, comes a new, caffeinated version of the sticky treat called Cracker Jack'd. The San Francisco Chronicle reports the new snack will have about the same amount of caffeine as a 12-ounce soda. While non-profit health organizations and consumer groups are concerned about the effects of caffeinated treats on children, a spokesman for Cracker Jack'd said the product will only be marketed towards adults.

Babies who are given acetaminophen -- the moderate painkiller commonly known by its brand name, Tylenol -- may have an increased risk of developing symptoms of asthma in their early childhood years according to a new study. HealthNews reports that although the "statistical link" doesn't prove the drug causes airway trouble, it does suggest that acetaminophen could promote asthma and is a cause for future study.

Laughter may really be the best medicine, NBC reports, according to a small study out of Britain. Researchers found that laughing releases some of the same "feel-good" chemicals that exercise does, so an ongoing, hearty laugh can trigger a release of endorphins. "No doubt the pharmaceutical companies won't like it, but laughter would save on hospital bills," Robin Dunbar, PhD, a professor of evolutionary psychology at Oxford University who led the study, told NBC.

A new study shows there may be some science involved in maintaining a monogamous relationship, the Los Angeles Times reports. According to a new study, when men in monogamous relationships got a sniff of oxytocin (the same hormone that's released in early romance and childbirth), they put extra space between them and an attractive woman they'd just met. When single, heterosexual males were given a dose of the hormone, they maintained their same, closer distance to the woman.
