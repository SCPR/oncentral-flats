How obesity affects drivers and a sex survey: In health news today

Jan. 22, 2013, 9:09 a.m.

A new survey on sex and romance found that 29 percent of women and 44 percent of men are bothered that their partner isn't more romantic. (TechNopal/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

Want to see how bad the flu is in Southern California – in map form? KPCC has your back.

Nearly half of the children born between 2004 and 2008 are "undervaccinated," according to a new study – some because their parents decided to skip shots recommended by federal health officials altogether, and others because they received vaccinations late. That worries researchers, Reuters says, because if enough kids don't get vaccinated, schools or communities could be at risk for at risk for whooping cough, measles or other communicable disease.

Science World Report says a new study has found that the prevalence of doctor-diagnosed attention deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) has increased about 24 percent in the past decade – and a whopping 90 percent among black girls, in particular. That may be because black girls are receiving better diagnoses, noted the study's authors.

Yet another risk of obesity has been uncovered by researchers, reports the New York Times: Obese drivers are more likely than their normal-weight counterparts to die in a car accident. It's possible their extra body fat prevents their seat belts from fitting as tightly, noted the study's authors, thus putting them at higher risk should a crash occur.

USA Today reports on a new international survey on what's "normal" in human sexuality and relationships. Among its findings: In the U.S., 29 percent of women and 44 percent of men say it bothers them "a lot" that their partner isn't more romantic.

People's jobs may affect their asthma risk, says a new British study, which found that one in six cases of the respiratory condition was linked to the workplace. HealthDay says jobs involving cleaning, or cleaner agents, had the strongest link to asthma – and farming quadrupled a person's risk for developing the condition.

CNN reports on a link between hearing loss and deteriorated thinking and memory skills. Researchers claim that people who experience hearing loss have a decline in thinking abilities up to 40 percent greater than those who don't lose any of their hearing capacity.

Speaking of sensory loss: According to Los Angeles Times: A new study says not getting enough sleep may make it more difficult to appreciate your loved ones – not to mention make it more difficult for them to be around you.
