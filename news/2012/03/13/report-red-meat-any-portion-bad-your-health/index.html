Report: Red meat, in any portion, is bad for your health

March 13, 2012, 3:30 p.m.

(Credit: Flickr via 46137)


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

In a new study released this week by the Harvard School of Public Health, even a small amount of red meat consumption was directly linked to an increased chance of death from cancer, cardiovascular disease and other health complications.

The study observed the life and dietary patterns of 37,698 men and 83,644 women for more than 20 years, and found that eating less than half a serving of red meat per day could have helped prevent a number of the subjects' deaths.

The Los Angeles Times reports that adding a daily serving of processed red meat, like a hot dog, to a person's diet, was linked to a 20 percent higher risk of death during the study.

In South L.A., red meat is a staple at many taco trucks, fast-food restaurants and mom-and-pop stores. In 2010, KPCC reported that fast-food restaurants made up nearly 71.8 percent of South L.A. eateries and included McDonald's and Jack in the Box, best known for their Big Mac and Jumbo Jack burgers.

At Tacos El Gavilán on Central Avenue and Washington Boulevard, their most popular dish is the carne asada quesadilla. At Guerrero Brothers Taco Truck on the corner of 21st and San Pedro streets, their most popular items are the carne asada tacos and burritos. These beef dishes aren't cooked in oil and are typically served in moderate portions -- but are they still unhealthy?

An Pan, a postdoctoral fellow at the Harvard School of Public Health in Boston, says yes. He told the L.A. Times that that there was no amount of red meat that's good for you.

"If you want to eat red meat, eat the unprocessed products, and reduce it to two or three servings a week," he said.

The American Meat (AMI) Institute disagrees and said the recent Harvard study took an "imprecise approach" because they relied heavily on the "notoriously unreliable self-reporting" of its subjects in regards to what they ate.

“Red and processed meat continues to be a healthy part of a balanced diet and nutrition decisions should be based on the total body of evidence, not on single studies that include weak and inconsistent evidence and stand in contrast to other research and to the Dietary Guidelines for Americans, 2010, " said Betsy Booren, foundation director of scientific affairs at AMI.

In South L.A., diet and exercise are ongoing issues. In 2010, they were addressed head-on at the governmental level when a ban on new, stand-alone fast food restaurants in the area was passed. Councilwoman Jan Perry said her goal with the ordinance was not trying to limit food options, but merely create more room for healthier restaurants and grocery stores.
