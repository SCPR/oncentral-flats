Buses and shuttles do little to help Vernon Central shoppers

Aug. 16, 2011, 10:46 a.m.


By Paige Osburn
OnCentral.org
1 comment

Tweet

A glance beyond the parking lot outside Family Farms Market on Central Avenue tells an interesting story.

An old man rides a bike with two milk gallons hanging from plastic bags on the handlebars. A family of three walk in the opposite direction; each parent carries two bags while their son walks with a watermelon, his stroller taken up by bulk cereal boxes. Two sisters stroll out of the parking lot with their shopping cart and head down Central Avenue.

The one thing these shoppers have in common is that none of them own a car.

“We have a pick-up,” acknowledged Emilio Gutierrez, 45. “But it’s in for repairs this week. Fourth time!”

They live 15 minutes away from the Family Farms, near 43rd Street and Honduras.

“Sure, it’s hot, we’re sweaty, he doesn’t like [to walk],” said Sara, 42, gesturing to their son. “But what choice do you have?”

For the quarter of low-income Angelenos who don't own a car, it turns out you don't have much of one. Many shoppers who find themselves carless are finding that help transporting their groceries from store to home is scarce — and that’s forcing them to get creative.

“I got rid of my car last year,” said LaDonna Deakins, 33. After a Saturday shopping session at Superior Grocery on Central Avenue, Deakins methodically transfers her groceries from a shopping cart to a dark blue roller suitcase. “Too expensive. Gas and insurance and stuff. […] This [suitcase] works just as good as a shopping cart, without having to steal something.”

She wheels the luggage home with her every week. It's a 20 minute walk.

Valencia Montes de Oca, 67, said she once tried bringing a small cart filled with groceries on the bus — and was turned down.

“It just went by,” translated a fellow bus-taker, Mark, who happened to be waiting with her for the 358. “It was so hot, in the middle of the summer.”

She claimed to have waited an hour for a bus driver to take pity and stop. “Since then, no more groceries. My son drives me. Those drivers are terrible!”

Mark said something to her in Spanish — when pressed to translate, he shrugged as she scowled in his general direction.

“I told her they’re just doing their jobs. It too crowded, too much people, maybe its policy.”

“There is no policy,” said Jose Abouldo said, a spokesman for Los Angeles Metropolitan Transit. “To be specific, there is nothing — no hard rules. It’s just…common sense.”

The closest thing the metro has to an official grocery "rule" is their policy against bringing “bulky items” on a bus or train that will cause people to fall, trip, or overcrowd. It’s a safety hazard, said Abouldo — if something blocks the aisle and there’s a crash, people can go tripping and careening into the windshield.

“One to three bags, you’re pretty good,” he summed up, “but if you want to bring on a supermarket cart, you’re in trouble.”

Grocery Store Solutions

When asked if the Fresh & Easy store on Central has a shuttle, one of its stockboys looked blank.

“A what?”

The reaction at Superior is similar (“What’s it do?”), and once its purpose is described, all three Central Avenue supermarkets — Superior, Family Farms, Fresh and Easy — have the same suggestion: the bus.

“We sit in-between two bus lines,” said the Fresh & Easy stockboy, pointing outside. “The 53 and the 34.”

But among shoppers on the street, the bus suggestion is treated with immediate skepticism, if not outright scorn.

“Bring this on the bus?” said Monica Perez, 26, gesturing at a 24-roll bulk buy of toilet paper in the shopping cart she’d commandeered. “What, am I insane? That’s stupid, there’s no space.”

Teresa Flores and her son Julio, agreed, even as they stood with two Superior Groceries plastic bags each and waited for the 53 northbound.

“I go to Superior for things like milk, eggs,” she says, “but I would not go every day. I would not buy more than three, four bags. That is what Amigo’s [Amigo Market, 23rd and Hooper] is for.”

The closest supermarket to South Central with a shuttle service is the Ralph’s at 2600 S Vermont Avenue. It’s a ten minute walk from USC’s campus and only serves residents who live within a three mile radius.

Grocery shuttles are far from a common occurrence — both in Los Angeles and in other North American cities. Studies have shown that shuttle services can actually be money-makers for the stores that do employ them, breaking-even with their operating costs within two to five months.

However, most shuttles are contracted not from grocery stores for their customers, but from universities or colleges for their students.

In the meantime, shoppers like Julio Martel are forced to use their wits — and their bicycles.

“They always do these 10 for 10 juice deals,” he confided, referencing the Family Farms. “I use the big store for bargains. I don’t have the money for anything else.” He began to laugh. "Don't have the money or the car."
