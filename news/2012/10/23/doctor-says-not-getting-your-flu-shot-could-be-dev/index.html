Doctor says not getting your flu shot could be 'devastating'

Oct. 23, 2012, 9:52 a.m.

Taking 30 minutes to get a flu shot, said one doctor, could save you from having to take 10 or more sick days from work. (USACE Europe District/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

It's flu season, which means most medical professionals are telling anyone who will listen to get their flu shot.

But information from the Office of Minority Health, which is within the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, shows that not all ethnic groups are equal when it comes to flu vaccinations.

More specifically, the agency found that in 2009, elderly black folks were 30 percent less likely to have gotten their flu shot within the past year than their white counterparts.

The same also happened to be true for black seniors and their pneumonia shots in 2010.

"I'm not at all surprised by the statistics," said Dr. Juanita Watts, who works in family medicine at Kaiser Permanente's Los Angeles Medical Center. "We struggle all the time with trying to encourage African-Americans to get the flu shot."

There are a few reasons for that. First, she said, a lot of people think they don't need it, maybe because they've never had the flu. There's also a knowledge gap that plays a role.

"What I have found is that African-Americans may or may not understand the shot altogether," Watts said. "They think you can get the flu from getting the shot."

Which you can't, she explained. There are two forms of the shot: intranasal and injection. The intranasal form – which is a nasal spray – has a weakened form of the influenza virus in it, and the injection has a deactivated, inactive form of the virus. Neither will get you sick with the flu, said Watts. The only side effects you may see are some tenderness or swelling at the site of injection and, in some folks, a mild fever.

There are also cultural reasons for the ethnic disparity in vaccination rates said Watts, who believes that in the black community, "there is a distrust and some misperceptions about the flu shot."

"In general, there is kind of this distrust over anything the government pushes or enforces," she added.

There is a demographic, though, that Watts has no trouble convincing to get their flu shot: people who have had the flu.

"They know how devastating it can be," she said. "Once you have the flu, you become a believer."

Watts said there's a common perception that there isn't a whole lot of difference between getting the flu and coming down with a cold. She begs to differ.

"The cold and the flu are two very different, distinct illnesses," she said. With a cold, you may have a fever, some aches, some mild fatigue – all of which can be effectively combatted with hydration and rest. A cold comes gradually, said Watts – but the flu?

"When you are attacked with the flu, it's very abrupt – it clobbers you over the head," she said. And how: Symptoms include chills, shaking, high fever, muscle aches, severe headaches, loss of appetite, weight loss and dehydration. And the fatigue is so extreme, said Watts, that getting up to go to the bathroom will make you "feel like you got up and moved to another city."

A flu shot, said Watts, can prevent all of this. And if it doesn't prevent a person from getting the flu outright, it'll ensure that a vaccinated person gets a much milder version of the virus. Taking the 30 minutes it takes to get the shot could save you from having to take 10 or more sick days from work, she said.

And now's the time to get it.

"The reason that I think it's so important for you to get this flu shot right now before the weather changes and flu season really starts is this is the best way we can protect you from getting the flu," said Watts. "After the vaccine, it takes about two weeks for your body to build up the antibodies you need." You'll need to get one every year, she added.

A few notes: If you already have the flu or if you get the flu, be sure to get to the doctor as soon as you can – preferably within the first four days of your illness. They may be able to alleviate some of your misery. And, even if you get your flu shot, take preventive measures, since the virus is notoriously contagious: Wash your hands, wipe down surfaces that may have been touched by a sick person and cough into your elbow, lest you sicken a co-worker or family member.

Finally, Watts said folks who are allergic to eggs or have a blood disorder, HIV/AIDS, severe chronic lung problems, a history of asthma or any other chronic illness, like diabetes, may be prone to complications from the flu shot, so they should talk to a health provider before getting it.
