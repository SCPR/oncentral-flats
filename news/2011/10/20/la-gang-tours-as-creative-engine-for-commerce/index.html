L.A. Gang Tours as creative engine for commerce

Oct. 20, 2011, 4:28 p.m.


By Rodrigo Mejia
Comment

Tweet

Alfredo Lomas reaches out with his tattooed arms, bracing himself against a woman’s shoulder. Early in the morning, the founding member of the L.A. Gang Tour is exchanging stories and full-bodied laughter with the crowd around him, breaking down the barriers built by his former membership in South L.A.’s Florencia 13 gang. He’s also getting them ready to embark across the more notorious corners of Los Angeles.

Someone approaches the gathering — a black man with a cap bent backwards and pants slung low. Lomas breaks the chatter and focuses on him.

“Is that Joker? What’s up, dawg?” asked Lomas, running over to embrace him. Lomas turns to the small group behind him, “You know ‘dawg’ is a compliment, right?”

Joker, otherwise known as Charles Smith, is one of five guides who lend experience to the tour and have a history of incarceration or gang affiliation. For Smith, having been released recently on parole, the first issue was finding a job in a post-bust economy that offers little room for maneuvering.

“I was working, just doing any odd job I can do, trying to find a steady job,” says Smith, a member of the Grape Street Crips in Watts.

That’s where the L.A. Gang Tour came in.

“I started doing the tours as soon as I came home,” Smith said.

At its inception, the tour — which runs through the L.A. River, Slauson Park and the Watts Towers — was a means to generate an economy for those who didn’t fit the formula in the mainstream market, namely ex- and current gang members who could then focus on addressing rising violence in their communities.

“They told me, ‘We got no jobs. We want to change, but how can we help do that with no jobs?” Lomas said. “The idea was to start a gang tour to hire these guys.”

For Smith, it was a perfect match. “Before, I was already working with the community, so it was just giving me another outlet,” says Smith.

After getting involved with the tours, Smith saw his career options begin to broaden. He started toiling under busses, learning first how to paint them, then give tours on them and now, fix them.

The end goal, Lomas said, is to take members like Smith and send them back into their communities to quell violence, saving both time and money the city pours into South L.A. every year for gang-fighting programs and incarceration costs.

The program is not only a means for guides to use their stories as live audio-tours for the ‘gang-museum’ of L.A., but also a way of opening up maligned communities to the outside world.

Director of the Watts Towers Art Center, Rosie Lee Hooks, said the curtain around South L.A. prevents outsiders from visiting its corridors, whether it’s city residents or investors.

“Nobody knows about us because we are in the Watts community,” she says. “Everybody is afraid to come down here, as evidenced by these tours. But once they come, they find out that we are just as safe as any part of the city.”

The tour, as Lomas began to reveal along the bumpy route, is a way to use a community that has a long shadow of gangs and drugs to creatively form a legitimate vehicle of employment that then allows a trickling of funds into the areas they focus on, both through the tour and through increased awareness.

The tour hasn’t been without its criticism; some have called it exploitative and equated it with a “slum tour.”

Lomas is quick to refute those claims. “We’re very clear on our approach into the communities. We don’t go into areas of the actual housing projects and we pass through main arteries. Those who criticize throw those comments from outside the community,” he says.

For those clicking away with their cameras along Alameda Avenue, the tour presents itself as a lens to sense the full scale of the city.

“It doesn’t seem like there’s much of a middle class, you’re either in gangs or in Beverly Hills,” says Blake Gregory, a tourist from Sydney, Australia who took the tour to get a full sense of the city. “I can’t really see one side without seeing the other side."
