Finding hidden value: New business owners see potential in Central Avenue

June 30, 2011, 8:51 p.m.

Soleyman Bababeygy makes and fills prescription glasses at his store, 20/20 Vision.


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

Standing tall, shiny and proud amid the dreary stretches of industry and looming vacant storefronts, a shopping complex on Central Avenue is fighting back against a bleak economy. Crowned by a Superior Grocer, this petite strip mall also hosts an optometrist, pharmacy and a new eyewear store, 20/20 Central Vision Care.

“I don’t make a lot of money here because the people, this area, are poor. This isn’t Beverly Hills,” said Soleyman Bababeygy, owner of the eyewear store.

And he should know, because Bababeygy and his wife live across town in Beverly Hills adjacent, but chose to open a store in Vernon Central because the demand and necessity were there.

Driving to and from his chemical plant every day, Bababeygy realized there was no eyewear store within a two-mile radius of the neighborhood and he recognized the capacity to not only turn a profit, but also help a population.

“One time he told me I want to help these people,” said Roya Bababeygy, of her husband. “These people need help. They don’t make enough money, they’re hard workers, maybe this way we are able to help them.”

Bringing a start-up business into a poor, urban neighborhood like Vernon Central is a positive, but unusual, turn in Los Angeles commerce, according to a 2008 study of the city spearheaded by Mayor Antonio Villaraigosa.

This DrillDown report was a comprehensive, in-depth look at nine Los Angeles neighborhoods, six in South Los Angeles and two in Central and East L.A.

What each area has in common is “untapped buying power” and “retail leakage,” according to the report. Because of negative media coverage and inaccurate census reporting, many investors and redevelopers overlook these neighborhoods’ potential, the report said.

This creates a cycle of poverty that has halted business development and caused consumers in these areas to take their purchasing power elsewhere – spending a large portion of their money in communities outside of their own.

Instead of buying groceries at the corner store or depositing paychecks at a local bank, residents may travel miles away to conduct their business.

Lynda Wilson, a Los Angeles native and a Central Avenue business owner, said that recently Central Avenue is on an upswing, but people have to understand the community and its demographic to build a successful business.

“You have to know the history,” Wilson said. “Once you know the history therein lies the value.”

Wilson grew up in the Vernon Central neighborhood and looks to the bustling commerce of the ‘30s, ‘40s and ‘50s as a reminder of Central Avenue’s potential. She comes from a long line of entrepreneurs, from grocery store owners to printers to beauticians, and being self-employed in her family is a necessity.

“When someone controls your purse strings they control your destiny,” Wilson said.

Wilson plans to open a Box Brothers store in September and chose her new business because it was “recession proof, depression proof.” As residents continue to move out of their houses, they will always need boxes, she said.

Wilson had her first ballet recital as a child and used to attend church just doors down from her new business, and her family has been stakeholders in the area for over 100 years. Wilson’s vested interest in the community makes her no less a businesswoman, but gives her a better grasp on what her neighbors need.

Bababeygy and 20/20 Central Care Vision also straddle this line between earning an income and offering a community service. To stay afloat, he admits that he’s had to slash prices to accommodate customers.

“The nature of this business is a helping business. Not only take. We give also,” Bababeygy said.

20/20 Vision opened only three weeks ago, but Bababeygy said he will work hard for two years and if the business succeeds, he will open another branch ten blocks away.

“They deserve to have a better life here,” Roya Bababeygy said. “This is just a good beginning.”
