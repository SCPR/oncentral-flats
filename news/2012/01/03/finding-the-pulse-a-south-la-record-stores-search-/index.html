Record store owner works to keep South LA's beat going

Jan. 3, 2012, 2:04 p.m.


By Alex Stillman
Comment

Tweet

A boy of no more than 14 years old walks nervously into the fluorescent glow of Bombass Muzik. It's after dark in South Los Angeles; the record store is one of the few bright spots on the block, the only other light coming from a "Liquidation Store" next door. He walks up to the counter and asks Serge Ureta, the store's owner, what he can do with some tracks he's put together.

"It's not like a real mixtape, just some stuff," the kid says.

"Bring it in! We'll play it!" Ureta exclaims. "That's what we're looking for, that drive, that motivation," he says.

And he will play it. Ureta is in the process of launching a record label, and has his eyes and ears wide open for new talent.

"M-C's gotta work five times as hard now," chimes in Grammy-winning, platinum-album-earning DJ U-Neek, producer of Bone Thugs N' Harmony, a regular at the store and Ureta's partner in the record label.

Ureta is a veteran in the L.A. hip-hop scene. He grew up as a breakdancer and MC in South LA and has run this record store for 14 years, a lone bulwark against the collapse of the independent record industry in South L.A. He DJs actively in the area and has a keen ear for talent. But talent has been scarce lately.

Hip-hop in LA is experiencing something of a rebirth. The sounds of the Dirty South have dominated radio airplay in recent years and popular themes have shifted. Popular rap's focus on gang and street life has moved on to party life, leaving a vacuum in the rap-cultural space once occupied by L.A. heroes NWA and Ice-T.

Parents who grew up with these voices are passing them on to the next generation, which is struggling to reconcile these sounds of the past with the climate of the present.

Ureta is in the middle of the tumult, listening to it happen and following closely. When asked about the rising young voices of South LA, he launches into a litany of names, most of which are nowhere near the radio right now. Tyga. Y-G. G-Malone. K-Boy. But he also names some shining stars, near-ubiquitous names like the New Boyz (godfathers of the "Jerking" movement) and skate-rap censor-bating critical darling Tyler, the Creator.

"The community evolved around hip-hop. It's more than just music, it's a culture," Ureta says. "Things were bad in the 90's with the gangster rap, but they're not all better. These kids still have stories to tell."

His job now is to help them tell their stories. He is rounding up artists, networking with radio stations, DJs and studio owners. He's looking for radio play for the voices of the community, those who live and breathe L.A. hip-hop.

The store is almost bursting with racks of CDs, tapes, and vinyl. It's also stocked with XXL t-shirts in white, black, blue — staples of neighborhood style. He's beginning to focus more on the apparel aspect of his business, which is currently propping up his flagging record sales.

But that is not all he does with his time.

After awhile, Ureta disappears into the back of the shop and soon the whirring of a circular saw starts.

One of Ureta's employees runs an after-school tutoring center out of the building next door, and Ureta, who provides more than half of the funding, is helping to renovate the space. The nearest Boys and Girls Club is on Vermont, out of walking distance of much of the neighborhood. They had been incredibly successful with the students that had come in and have turned underachievers into students-of-the-month.

The community is obviously important to Ureta, and he's working hard to keep it on its feet — through music and anything else that comes his way.

Bombass Muzik is open from 10:00-8:30, M-F, 10:00-7:00 Sat, and 10:00-5:30 Sunday. They stream live daily from noon to 4:00pm on http://stickam.com/bombassmuzik.
