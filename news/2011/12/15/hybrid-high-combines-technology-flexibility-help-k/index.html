‘Hybrid High’ combines technology, flexibility to help kids stay in school

Dec. 15, 2011, 6 a.m.

This conceptualized street view was done by Rodolfo Ramirez, one of 12 students in the Design Studio of the USC School of Architecture, who participated in a process to imagine different learning environments that could reflect the Hybrid High School concept.


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

USC is going hybrid, combining online curriculum and face-to-face instruction in a new LAUSD charter high school aimed at lowering the drop-out rate. Hybrid High, expected to open next fall, will be open year-round, seven days a week for up to 12 hours a day. The long hours and the flexible class scheduling are aimed at accommodating students who struggle to graduate while holding down a job or taking care of family members.

The school will be specifically aimed at students who are at a high risk for dropping out, and features like varying models of teaching like online coursework and weekly meetings with academic advisors can bend to students varying schedules.

David Dwyer is the executive director of Hybrid High. He likens the school's scheduling structure to an emergency room where there are no set hours and shifts change every week. In an emergency room, the expert doctor is not always needed, and the same goes for the classroom.

In addition to the seven full-time and one part-time credited teachers on staff, the school will employ graduate students, retired teachers and other instructional aides to work with students when credited teachers aren’t needed.

“The notions of the square box classroom and school don’t necessarily work for everyone; it’s an artifact,” said Melora A. Sundt, associate dean for academic programs and professor of clinical education at the USC Rossier School.

Dwyer said this flexibility and the use of “instructional teams” allows students to receive more one-on-one attention, whether in person on online.

Online class choices include high school standards like U.S. History, U.S.Government and Biology, but also include arts courses like Music Appreciation and practical application classes like Personal Finance.

All students are eligible to apply for Hybrid High and the same random lottery system that’s used for all charter schools will be used to select students for this new school. But Dwyer said Hybrid will try to “load the ballot box” in order to attract the type of students the school is aimed at.

“I think we’ll have a heterogenous group, which is actually a good thing but we definitely want to reach the student population that we’re concerned about,” Dwyer said.

They intend to reach this group by focusing on middle schools in the neighborhoods surrounding USC and doing most of their marketing through community organizations that serve lower socioeconomic classes.

According to a USC video, 1.2 million students in the U.S. drop out of school every year. Dwyer said this school is not a drop-out recovery program, but a program aimed at preventing dropouts and targeting students who are still in middle school.

Although the program will start small with a class of 150 ninth-graders, enrollment is expected to expand to 650 within four years of the school being opened.

“We've really enjoyed the program sort of being adopted by not just the School of Education, but other [USC] schools," Dwyer said.

The Rossier School of Education established the non-profit Ednovate to be responsible for the day-to-day operations of the charter school. USC's school of architecture is helping re-imagine the use of space for the construction of Hybrid High and the school of communication is considering producing a documentary that chronicles the triumphs and failures of the school's formative years.

The charter school will be taking applications up until the end of March and the drawing of names will be held at the beginning of April. The LAUSD approved the school’s charter earlier this month, paving the way for official opening next September in a still to be determined renovated space near USC’s campus.
