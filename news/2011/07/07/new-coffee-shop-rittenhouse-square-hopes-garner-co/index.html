Boyle Heights coffee shop owner hopes to inspire at new location in Rittenhouse Square

July 7, 2011, 12:42 p.m.

New Primera Taza Coffe House awaits final inspections before it opens in July.


By Jessica Perez
1 comment

Tweet

CORRECTION: This story was changed August 18, 2011 to accurately reflect the name of the CRA. It is the Community Redevelopment Agency, not the California Redevelopment Agency.

Primera Taza Coffee House hosts poetry readings, comedy nights and art exhibits at its Eastside location. Locals drop in for a latte and stay for a conversation. Across the street, vibrant sounds and smells project from the Boyle Heights Farmers Market, pushed into existence with owner Juan Romero's help last year.

“This is a peaceful place where people can celebrate our community when it comes to our cultural history expressed through the arts,” said photographer and long-time Boyle Heights resident Eddie Ruvalcaba.

Last year, Ruvalcaba had his first-ever photo exhibit at Primera Taza, a project he says was inspired by Romero. He explains the recent art revival continues to change the neighborhood, trading a drug-infested intersection for a cultural experience and active nightlife along First Street.

“People are not afraid to come out to the streets. It’s such a different environment,” Ruvalcaba said.

That same sound of revival is what city officials hope for the Central Avenue Corridor, merging the old with the new by bringing improvements to the area while still preserving its legacy.

This month, Romero will open his second location in Rittenhouse Square, the mixed-use affordable senior housing complex at 33rd and Central Avenue. The building is part of the Central Avenue Action Plan, a project approved by the Community Redevelopment Agency of Los Angeles (CRA/LA) in 2005.

“This is a community that has a real emphasis on culture and the history of culture and music,” said Jenny Scanlin, regional director for the CRA/LA. “These investments are mainly to revitalize the neighborhood.”

During the 1940s and '30s, Central Avenue was home to a thriving jazz scene. Today’s demographics are different, with small shops and restaurants catering to the neighborhood’s low-income Latino majority — mirroring that of Boyle Heights'.

With other redevelopment projects already realized along Central Avenue, Scanlin says CRA/LA hopes to recreate what Primera Taza has done on the Eastside: provide a gathering space and encourage community engagement.

“Primera Taza is more than just a coffee shop,” Scanlin said. “It’s nice to have a another small indoor venue where the community can share their culture and individuals can share their talent.”

Revitalization has also been a focus in Boyle Heights. With the 2009 arrival of the Metro Gold Line extension came investment surrounding a blossoming arts district along the First Street corridor, where Primera Taza now sits.

“It feels great to be in the middle of this. We’ve been very fortunate to be able to highlight local artists at Primera Taza,” Romero said.

Primera Taza opened in July 2009, hoping to bring a fairly new concept to his community. It wasn’t long before the coffee shop drew locals, nearby professionals and visitors dropped off by the recent metro rail extension who enjoy not just a cup of coffee but partake in the cultural community activity in and around the coffee shop.

Although Romero will now be new guy on the block, he says he has already began creating partnerships in South Los Angeles because community input is what will mark his coffee shop’s success.

“I want to give opportunity for the community to bring the flavor of South L.A… the different ethnic groups and nonprofits, and collaborate with them because I want to make this a community coffee shop,” said Romero.
