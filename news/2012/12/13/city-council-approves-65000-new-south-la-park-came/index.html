City Council approves $65,000 for new South LA park cameras

Dec. 13, 2012, 12:48 p.m.

Ross Snyder Park will receive four new cameras to help discourage crime.


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

This week the L.A. City Council approved the allocation of $65,000 for four new surveillance cameras to be installed at a South L.A. Park. Officials hope the use and monitoring of these cameras will deter crime in the area as well as help the LAPD make arrests when they do occur.

The new cameras are slated for Ross Snyder Park, located on 41st Street near Compton Avenue. Although conditions have improved in this area in recent years, this park has had a history of crime and gang activity. The Gang Reduction and Youth Development (GRYD) program identified the area around Ross Snyder as a district where violent gang-related crime is more than 400 percent higher than elsewhere in the city.

This park was subsequently chosen as one of the locations for the City's Summer Night Lights initiative -- a five-year-old program that aims to reduce gang violence during the summer months when crime often spikes.

Councilwoman Jan Perry, who led the charge for the new cameras, said she's grateful there was no specific "precipitating event" that led to the new cameras, but rather it is a project they have been working on for a while. She said the cameras will provide "sweeping views" of the park and be monitored by the local LAPD Newton division.

The councilman added that when surveillance cameras were installed years ago at South Park on 51st and Avalon streets, there was a palpable change in the community.

"Within the first month, the park had begun to turn a corner to be a far more hospitable place for children and families,‚Äù said Perry, adding that in that time the LAPD also made 100 felony arrests.

But Perry's district isn't the only one turning to surveillance cameras to help thwart gang activity. Councilman Bernard Parks of the neighboring district has been working to get cameras installed in Harvard Park, the site of a September shooting that left a 19-year-old dead. The Los Angeles Times reports that the cameras were supposed to be installed in August but had been held up in a council committee for months.

Harvard Park had been taken over and run by a gang for years, but recently had begun to show marked signs of improvement -- Parks told the L.A. Times that cameras would be a crucial step forward in making the community feel safe.

Soon after the shooting the city council did approve the installation of cameras in the park. Parks told NBC the cameras would be "bulletproof" and "tamper-proof."
