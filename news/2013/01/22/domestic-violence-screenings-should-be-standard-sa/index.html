Domestic violence screenings should be standard, says expert panel

Jan. 22, 2013, 12:50 p.m.

A panel of experts has released a new report recommending that all clinical workers screen their patients for domestic violence, whether or not they show signs or symptoms. (Alex Proimos/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

A panel of experts has recommended that health care providers screen all women of childbearing age for signs of "intimate partner violence," even if they show no signs or symptoms of abuse.

In a report appearing in the Annals of Internal Medicine, the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) cites "adequate evidence that available screening instruments can identify current and past abuse or increased risk for abuse."

"Intimate partner violence" was defined as physical, sexual, or psychological harm inflicted upon a current or former partner. It can take place in heterosexual or same-sex couples and doesn't require sexual intimacy.

The report pointed to strong evidence that detecting abuse early can lead to "effective interventions" which "can reduce violence, abuse, and physical or mental harms for women of reproductive age" – in this case, those between the ages of 14 and 46.

Those interventions are complex and have many components, but just giving people a chance to get to that point is a major accomplishment, said Adriana Molina, the co-chair of the Violence Prevention Coalition of Greater Los Angeles.

"The first step that's important is asking the question and giving people permission to acknowledge that they are at risk or that they have a concern, that they don't feel safe" she said. "Just opening the door."

It was with "moderate certainty" that the USPSTF concluded that screening women for intimate partner violence "has a moderate net benefit." Researchers used the language of moderation because screening for this kind of violence is only one important, if not small, aspect of prevention, but Molina said the report certainly isn't something to be dismissed.

"You think about being moderately certain that something is going to happen, it's a pretty good chance," she said. "It's not just a slight chance. What [the USPSTF] is talking about is they're feeling like asking the questions will have a significant impact on our ability as a society to provide the necessary services."

Molina added that "we don't typically talk in our society about domestic violence and where to go for help." Making the medical community a more integral part of the resource network for those who are abused could help put more people in touch with that help.

"There are so many people who have permission from an abusive partner to go to a doctor, where they wouldn't have permission to go to a counseling center or community-based agency," said Molina.

The task force's screening recommendation applies to all women of childbearing age, although it does note certain factors that increase a women's risk of being abused: youth, substance abuse, marital problems and economic hardship, to name several.

The report says that intimate partner violence is underreported and "largely unaddressed." Nearly two-thirds of women and one-fourth of men report having experienced some sort of violence at the hands of their partner at least once, and in 2005, nearly 24 percent of women reported a "lifetime history of suffering abuse."

"It's really challenging to know the depth of underreporting that's happening," said Molina. There's a slew of reasons for people to be afraid of reporting intimate partner violence, including fear of deportation if legal authorities become involved. Social workers will often "discover" domestic violence only because it's a risk factor in separate cases primarily revolving around children. For others, this sort of violence has become normalized, because it's how they grew up and all they've ever known.

The USPSTF noted that it did "not consider the costs of providing a [screening] service in this assessment." That cost, said Molina, would probably mostly lie in training health providers on how to ask the right questions in the right way.

"It's not just about asking the question, but how you ask the questions and what you are looking for in terms of responses, in terms of language patterns, in terms of body language, in terms of who's in the room while you're asking," she said.

The screenings are just a tool, said Molina. "But just like a hammer, it's great in the hands of someone who spends a lot of time hammering."

While the task force noted that the elderly, as well as "physically or mentally dysfunctional" adults also have a higher risk of being abused or neglected, there wasn't enough data to conclude whether screening them routinely would be particularly effective.
