SCOTUS decision on health care reform: Most won't like it

June 20, 2012, 2:08 p.m.

No matter what the Supreme Court decides regarding the Affordable Care Act, most Americans won't like it. (Credit: Joe Gratz/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

"Consensus" is most certainly not the word when it comes to a decision on health care reform.

Just ask the Pew Research Center, which released a survey earlier this week that says no matter what the Supreme Court decides regarding the Affordable Care Act (ACA), the public will not be satisfied.

That means that whether the nine Supreme Court justices decide to uphold the entire law, reject the individual mandate or strike down the whole thing, less than half of the American people will be happy with their decision.

The Supreme Court is currently reviewing the ACA on grounds that it is unconstitutional. A decision is expected sometime this month.

By the numbers:

– Were the entire law to be thrown out: 44 percent would approve of the decision, while 48 percent would not.

– If the individual mandate were struck down and the rest of the law upheld: 40 percent would be happy, while 51 percent would not.

– If the entire ACA makes it through: 39 percent will be happy, and 51 percent will not.

Pew notes that although overall more disapprove of the law (48 percent) than approve (43 percent), only about one-fifth of Americans have a good understanding of the law; conversely, about one-third admit they don't understand the law very well or well at all.

Brietta Clark, a professor of law at Loyola Law School who specializes in health care law and access issues, says it's important for polls to pay special attention to nuance. She pointed to one example.

"People are extremely happy they have Medicaid as opposed to being uninsured," she said. "We know that. We have surveys that tell us that. But we also know that people on Medicaid have trouble accessing certain kinds of care because the payment system is so low." And so, she said, she's seen polls that take that and conclude that people are unhappy with Medicaid as a whole.

"It is nuanced, and polls [often] don't capture this nuance," she said.

The survey also looked at partisan reactions to possible court rulings:

– 75 percent of Republicans will be happy if the entire law is overturned, while 62 percent of Democrats will approve of the court's upholding the ACA.

– Throwing out the individual mandate and keeping the rest of the law isn't appealing to either liberals or conservatives: 47 percent of Republicans and 56 percent of Democrats will be unhappy with that outcome.

– Independents would be happiest if the entire law was thrown out.

Many partisans were – well, just that: 40 percent of Republicans would only be happy if the entire law is overturned, and 39 percent of Democrats would only be happy if the entire is upheld.

The survey also measured respondents' attitudes toward Arizona's immigration legislation that is also awaiting a decision from the Supreme Court, SB 1070.
