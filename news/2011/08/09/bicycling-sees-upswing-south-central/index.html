More bikes: Bike businesses and advocacy on the upswing in South Los Angeles

Aug. 9, 2011, 4:50 p.m.

Mendez Bike Shop at Central and Adams


By Paige Osburn
OnCentral.org
Comment

Tweet

Walk down Central Avenue on a summer’s day and you’ll see not one, not two, but seven bike shops. They run from Mother’s at 79th all the way up to Mendez Bike Shop at Central and Adams, run by Florencio Mendez and his wife, Irma. Piles of chains, gears and detached wheels litter the one-room store but racks of bikes line the walls and the sidewalk outside.

“Our big sell now is the fixie,” says Irma, pointing to a lime-green bike on the wall. “But when we start it was low-riders, the G-2—the BMX kind—and now this one.”

They opened shop in 1994, after being forced to leave their first location at Western and Washington.

“When we start, it was very difficult,” Irma explains. “But then they start knowing us and six years ago things start to pick up.”

Biking is seeing growth in South Los Angeles — from CicLAvia swinging through to increase protections for bicyclists, the business of biking is good.

Now, she claims, they have too much business — people coming and going at all hours of the day. Mendez has been repairing bicycles with his family since he was eight years old.

“I can do things the rest can’t,” he says, smiling. But he also can’t deny that cycling is simply more popular. “Used to be we just get kids. Now, too many ages. We get kids, but also adults, students. More people need bikes.”

According to the 2006 census, of the nearly five million workers aged 16 or older in Los Angeles County, 72 percent commuted to work driving alone, while only 11.9 percent drove in a carpool and 7.0 percent commuted on public transportation. Less than 1 percent commuted by bike. But, argues Damien Newton, times are changing.

“In the past four years, we’ve really started to see a shift,” says Newton, creator of LA Streetsblog, which devotes itself to tracking and promoting Los Angeles public transit. “More people are bicycling, whether by choice or by high gas prices — we’re seeing a real spike.”

The evidence is there. Sen. Alan Lowenthal’s proposed legislation requiring a 3-foot berth before a car passes a bicycle is heading to its final vote. This week Metrolink introduced railcars outfitted with space for at least 18 bicycles instead of just two. And Mayor Antonio Villaraigosa has championed the cycling cause, pledging 10 percent of the voter approved Measure R to cycling renovations.

“That doesn’t seem like a lot,” Newton said, “but 10 percent of $40 billion is $60 to 90 million. Paint on bike lanes don’t cost a ton of money.”

A 2010 study by the UCLA shows that the person-to-car ratio for South Los Angeles — 0 to 0.2 — is smaller than other LA regions, where the ratio averages 0.6-0.8 or higher . However, studies which refer specifically to bikes are notoriously rare.

“There’s very little in the way of hard data,” Newton said. “We mostly rely on anecdotal evidence. How many bikes you see in bike racks, how many men go by on bicycles wearing, you know, khakis and polo shirts, backpacks. They’re using their bikes to get to their jobs.”

Candelario Ramos, 45, of DC Bike Shop (four blocks down, at Central and 29th) agrees.

“People buy bikes to go to work,” he says, as the owner’s son Juan translates. “We get customers of all ages now. Eighteen to forty.”

DC Bike Shop has been open only a year, but business has apparently been booming.

“We get twenty, thirty customers a week,” says Ramos. “Times are changing. Times are good for bikes.”
