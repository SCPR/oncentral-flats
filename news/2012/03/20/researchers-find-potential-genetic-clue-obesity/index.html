Researchers find potential genetic clue to obesity

March 20, 2012, 7:48 a.m.

Researchers found a genetic mutation in mice that prevented the body's "stop eating" message from properly reaching the brain. (Credit: Tatiana Bulyonkova/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

Researchers from Georgetown University Medical Center believe they have identified a genetic mutation that can lead to obesity.

The study, which was published in Nature Medicine, found that within mice with this particular mutation, the body's "stop eating" message was blocked.

The BBC explains that mice with the mutation consumed up to 80 percent more food than is normal. Hormones are supposed to tell the brain when the body is full, but within mice with this mutation – and, researchers are suggesting, perhaps humans – this message wasn't being passed to the correct part of the brain.

Thus, explained researcher Baoji Xu to BBC, "the appetite is not modified."

Xu said that this genetic discovery could "open up novel strategies to help the brain control body weight." Genetics, added University of Cambridge professor Sadaf Farooqi, "have a surprisingly large role." She told BBC that between 40 and 70 percent of the difference in weight between two people is genetically rooted.

The discovery is especially pertinent – and maybe hopeful – for South Los Angeles, which has some of the worst obesity rates in the county. L.A. City Councilwoman Jan Perry's Ninth District was found to have an adult and child obesity rate of 36.7 and 29.5 percent, respectively, according to statistics released in 2011 by the L.A. County Department of Public Health.

That's exacerbated by the lack of healthy food options in the area.
