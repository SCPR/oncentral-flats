'Know the Signs' campaign aims to prevent suicide in California

Dec. 17, 2012, 1:48 p.m.

"Know the Signs" is a state-wide campaign to help prevent suicide. (Meredith Farmer/Flickr Creative Commons)


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

A statewide marketing campaign dubbed "Know the Signs" aims to help prevent suicide through education. In 2010 there were 38,364 suicides in the U.S., making it the tenth leading cause of death across the country. "Known the Signs" is designed to teach people how to recognize the warning signs of suicide, how to talk with someone who's in crisis and then where to go to find the necessary professional help.

The Los Angeles Times reports that the state-wide campaign is part of a $30-million suicide prevention initiative that began in 2011 and will continue until June 2014. The "Know the Signs" website was launched in October but the media effort was just announced last week, according to the L.A. Times.

The campaign's media effort will include English and Spanish TV spots, outdoor billboards and print ads, according to a press release. It will not only help inform the public on available suicide prevention resources, but work with the news media to "increase reporting about suicide in ways that are consistent with national recommendations."

The "Know the Signs" website includes a list of indicators that may mean your friend, family member or colleague is considering suicide. In addition to actually talking about suicide or wanting to die, signs of suicide include giving away possessions, increased alcohol or drug use, anger, anxiety, changes in sleep or withdrawal.

“While the signs for suicide can be subtle, they are there,” said Anara Guard, suicide prevention advisor to the campaign, in a statement. “The campaign goals are to educate people on what those signs are, to recognize them when they are present, and how to intervene early before the situation becomes critical.”

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), in 2011 more than 15 percent of U.S. students between 9th and 12th grades reported that they had seriously considered attempting suicide during the year preceding the survey. "Know the Signs" says that teens considering suicide may show signs including neglect of personal appearance, physical pain or sudden changes in mood.

South Los Angeles actually has some of the lowest suicide rates in L.A. County. According to statistics from the L.A. County Department of Public Health, there were only 3.5 deaths per 100,000 people in South L.A. in 2009. James Cunningham, the head of Mental Health Clinical Program in the county's Department of Mental Health, wrote in an email that in L.A. County, "you will find that rates of suicide are related to socioeconomic status, that is to say that low-income populations generally have lower suicide rates."
