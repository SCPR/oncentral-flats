Under the radar: Bienestar offers a home to the LGBT community

April 5, 2012, 1:32 p.m.

Adrian Franco, far right, facilitates the LGBT youth support group on Wednesday nights at Bientestar's South L.A. location. Bienestar is the only LGBT resource center in the area. (Credit: Brian De Los Santos/OnCentral)


By Brian De Los Santos
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

The building at 130 West Manchester Avenue looks like a small house, adjacent to a Roscoe’s Chicken and Waffles and shopping centers. It doesn’t resemble anything special, in particular, but within its walls it hosts an lesbian/gay/bisexual/transgender (LGBT) resource center – the only one in the South L.A. area.

Bienestar (“well-being” in Spanish) South L.A. is presently the only LGBT resource center in the South LA community that offers free services other than the AIDS Project Los Angeles (APLA) in the west side of L.A., said Roberto Gómez, center supervisor at Bienestar's South LA branch.

The umbrella organization serves the LBGT community in South L.A. and at other seven locations including East L.A., where it was established in 1989. Gómez said the social justice non-profit addresses different LGBT issues aimed at the “underserved” Latino community, including health, support groups, advocacy, free HIV testing and syringe exchange, and research. Most of their financial support is aggregated from federal, state and local agencies, and although they have lost personnel and offices due to loss of funding, they continue to serve the community.

They facilitate most of their groups and workshops in Spanish but Gómez said they try to accommodate everyone who is interested. He added that they have participants from different backgrounds and also welcome straight allies. Many folks, however, seem to shy away from asking for help or participating in free services at the center.

“Often times people will call Bienestar ‘the HIV clinic’. We are not a HIV clinic. But people associate us with that, as the gay center or HIV clinic. That stigma will keep people away,” Gómez said.

The stigma, he said, is one that says LGBT persons are “weak people, less than the rest.” He described the scene in South L.A. as “survival of the fittest,” and so community members, youth especially, prefer not to be seen attending meetings at an LGBT center.

Gina Perez, 17, attends the youth support group meetings on Wednesday nights. She recently told OnCentral that although she does not care for others’ opinions, she feels like she is misjudged and faces discrimination for being a gay teen.

Adrian Franco is a youth group facilitatory at Bienestar. “To be in South LA, you struggle with dual identities,” he said. “You’re either Latino or Black or any person of color and you’re dealing with your sexuality. It’s a little tougher for some of the youth to cope with.”

Bienestar coordinators said that many residents seek help in other neighborhoods like West Hollywood and take part in LGBT support communities there, because they feel safe.

Gómez said cultural barriers – especially religion – are a large part of the reason why residents go out of the area to seek help and support. “Often the church will not be accepting of [LBGT members], he said. “Those are very big factors.”

Tod M. Tamberg, director of media relations for the archdiocese of Los Angeles, said parishes are the contacts for counseling and resources for the LGBT community at a local level. However, the Catholic Church does not have an official resource center in South L.A., and Tamberg said the Archdiocese does not plan to create one.

When OnCentral asked Ruben Restrepo, who is the pastor at St. Vincent de Paul Church on South Figueroa Street and Adams Boulevard, about resources for LBGT members, he said there wasn’t really any at his parish. "We have a lot of things here. Maybe in the future, because we have few people and we need to support the few programs we have," he said in February.

And there's another reason that keeps people from getting involved with the LGBT community in South L.A., according to Franco.

“What I’ve noticed in South L.A. is that many of the youth and participants in my groups have a very strong feeling of internalized homophobia,” Franco said. “Where they themselves don’t feel comfortable with their sexuality, considering what they’re surrounded by: their families, and the culture in South L.A., is significantly different [than it would be in] other parts of LA.”

The negative views or stigma of South L.A. do not keep Franco or Gómez from serving in the community. Franco said he is appreciative of the work he gets to do at the center and has been impacted by the lives he counsels.

“At the end of the day, when you see [participants] walking away with a good piece of knowledge or something that they learned, it’s rewarding,” Franco said.

More information about Bienestar can be found at their website.
