Businesses prepare for Central Avenue Jazz Festival

July 28, 2011, 10:19 a.m.


By Jessica Perez
Comment

Tweet

Days before the biggest annual summer event on Central Avenue, 27th Street Bakery owner Jeanette Pickens and her husband prepare flyers and coupons in between pie orders and deliveries. For 15 years, they’ve seen how the Central Avenue Jazz Festival draws crowds by the thousands, and for the 16th year, they expect the same.

“[The festival] brings back the people that remember when Central Avenue was very busy, when it was a hub for African Americans and entertainers,” said Pickens who runs the bakery her grandparents opened in 1956.

Since the family business has sustained itself for decades, thriving on their popular home-style recipes, they never found it necessary to do much promotion at the festival. But after the bakery’s addition of an Internet café, Pickens said she thinks it's a good time to host a booth for her business and promote their goods and services with a community that might already know they exist, but may need a little reminder.

“We decided to expand our product line. We want to bring that knowledge to people, let them know that we’re here; we have Latino products, we have Southern products and an Internet café,” Pickens said.

This is a strategy Johnny Andrade, business liaison for the Central Avenue Business Association (CABA) with Coalition for Responsible Community Development (CRCD) has been pushing with Central Avenue business owners.

“One of the ways [the festival] helps businesses is that it brings a lot of new exposure. Everyone from L.A. County comes here,” Andrade said. “This is a day when they have to come out and really push their product.”

Many businesses have taken that advice. Andrade says there’s been a 50 percent rise in vendor booths registered by Central Avenue businesses this year. Along with CABA, CRCD advises business owners to decorate storefronts, bring out merchandise when possible and pass out flyers and coupons.

Bonnie Hayslett, owner of Bubbles and Clips Pet Groomers has learned to take advantage of opportunities like this. Although she knows not many festival-goers will be bringing their pets for grooming next weekend, she plans to join CABA at their booth and engage customers with raffles and coupons.

“I’ve picked up customers from the jazz festival because I talk to a lot of people. Anytime you have a big crowd, you have a chance for people to come meet you, it’s an opportunity to help brand yourself as a business on Central Avenue,” Hayslett said.

However, not all businesses see the potential behind the annual music event. Some say they will conduct business as usual and don’t predict they will be affected at all.

Joel Nava, manager of Mercado Garibaldi, a produce and meat market a few doors down from Central Avenue Jazz Park, says it’s hard to invest money for promotion when they constantly see a loss in profits.

“It’s dead around here on Friday, so we lose out on sales. If we get lucky, we get some foot traffic to come in during the weekend,” Nava said.

He mostly blames the Central Avenue street closures, taking effect Friday at 10 a.m. and reopening Sunday at 11 p.m.

Andrade said he is aware of Nava’s complaints and says streets closures are a challenge event planners are working to modify next year.
