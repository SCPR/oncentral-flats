Despite unique challenges, USC freshman stays in step and in tune

Jan. 30, 2012, 3:12 p.m.

Constantine Greanias is a 18-year-old tuba player in the USC marching band. Click on the audio below to hear him play The Fight Song on his double bell euphonium.


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

Constantine Greanias is a typical USC freshman. When I met him he was dressed in sandals, jeans and a red and gold Trojan t-shirt, carrying a backpack and a double bell euphonium (essentially a mini tuba), with surprising ease for such a cumbersome case.

This 18-year-old is in the school marching band and besides expertly executing the musical maneuvers, he navigates the sprawling USC campus like a seasoned senior -- he even described the school as "compact." In addition, he does it all blind.

I asked him how he learned the lay of the land so quickly. "Maybe just being happy here has helped," he said.

Greanias can still see the full spectrum of colors, but his vision is almost entirely blurred. The average person's eyesight fades out somewhere on the horizon line; for Greanias, that horizon line begins right in front of his face. He can normally begin to read items in a size 26 font or larger. That's twice the size of what you're reading right now.

He has a program on his cell phone that reads text and transmits incoming information. He uses e-textbooks and a computer-voiced reader to stay current in his classes and studying.

Greanias said he's gotten skilled at memorizing things quickly, especially as part of the marching band, since they cover a lot of campus ground.

He grew up in Valencia and was born with a "genetic defect" known as Stargardt disease -- an inherited juvenile macular degeneration. Essentially, this condition causes people to lose sight in their center field of vision. His family really started noticing the effects of Stargardt when he was about five years old. His eyesight continued to worsen until he was 12, when it stabilized. It's not expected to not get much worse.

"I'm not really sure if my visual impairment has made a difference in making music or not," Greanias said.

But, he added, most of the blind people he knows play instruments, and they play them well. He remains unconvinced that there's a correlation. For Greanias, it could just be practice: He did after all, first get involved in music at the age of six.

"My parents had me start learning piano like every good parent does, as far as I can tell," Greanias joked. In addition to piano, Greanias has played the oboe, tuba, harmonica, flute and guitar.

"Music is a getaway from reality for me," he said. "I love hearing the music I make. I love hearing other music."

Now, Greanias gets to hear both almost everyday. During the marching band's busy fall season, they practice four times a week, including a four-hour practice on Saturday and live performances at football games. Half his weekend is consumed by the marching band.

"I probably spend more time with my band friends than I do in class," he joked.

As a USC newbie, Greanias has freshman responsibilities -- meaning he must help clean everyone else's horns in the band.

For now, Greanias is enjoying the band's off season, playing his double bell euphonium in the dorm and looking forward to his new life in L.A.

"The city has a lot more to do then Valencia did," Greanias said. "I haven't gotten a chance to explore all of it but what I have seen has been really, really cool and I'm looking forward to seeing more in the coming semesters."
