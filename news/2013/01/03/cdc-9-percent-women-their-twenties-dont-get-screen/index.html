CDC: 9 percent of women in their twenties don't get screened for cervical cancer

Jan. 3, 2013, 4:33 p.m.

A woman receives the HPV vaccine. Federal health officials recommend that the three-shot series is given to all children once they turn 11 or 12, before they become sexually active. (VCU CNS/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

More than 12,000 women were diagnosed with cervical cancer in 2012, and upwards of 4,200 died of the disease that same year.

That cancer rate and death toll – along with the fact that the condition is preventable – is part of the reason that January is Cervical Cancer Awareness Month.

Women can be screened for cervical cancer one of two ways: a Pap test and an (human papillomavirus) HPV screen. The latter is the more familiar test, although there's been some confusion lately as to how often women should get one because the guidelines were recently modified. HealthDay, however, clarifies:

– No screening for women younger than 21.

– Women between 21 and 29 should be screened every three years.

– Women between 30 and 65 should be screened every three years or be receive both a Pap and HPV test every five years.

– Women older than 65 only need to be screened if they have a higher-than-normal cancer risk or if they haven't been screened regularly throughout their lives.

Note that these guidelines only apply to women who are healthy. HealthDay adds that all women are advised to have an annual pelvic exam, and should check with their health providers to figure out what screening schedule is best for them.

But new data from the Jan. 4 edition of the Morbidity and Morality Weekly Report, released by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), shows that among women in their twenties, who should be screened every three years, the percentage who reported never having a Pap test rose pretty significantly: from 6.6 percent to 9 percent.

"Public health initiatives to increase screening among these women should continue," wrote the report's authors.

In addition to these preventive measures, there's also an HPV vaccine, which has been recommended for both girls and boys when they turn 11 or 12 – that is, before they become sexually active. It can also be used as a protective measure once someone is sexually active or already has the virus, and it doesn't replace the need for screening.

Here's more on the CDC's National Breast and Cervical Cancer Early Detection Program, the goal of which is to provide access to screening services for both conditions across the U.S.
