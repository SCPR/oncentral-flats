Broadway Federal Bank closes two branches, but opens up to future opportunities

Oct. 12, 2011, 3:51 p.m.


By Brooklyne Gipson
Comment

Tweet

In 1946 Broadway Federal Bank quietly opened it’s doors at 4329 S. Broadway in Los Angeles. For eight years, the understated three room office served as headquarters for a group of men and women who catered to the underserved African-American population in Los Angeles.

Their mission: “providing conventional loans and training for minorities desirous of entering the [banking] industry.”

Throughout it’s tumultuous 65-year history, Broadway Federal has seen it’s fair share of ups and downs including the purchase of it’s flagship headquarters on 45th and Broadway in 1954 and the civil unrest in 1992 that saw the building burned to the ground, ironically, by the same community members that would need it’s services most in the aftermath of the uprising. Today, the resilient financial institution continues to carry out it’s mission in spite of it’s recent decision to close two of it’s branches.

According to the Los Angeles Times, the federal government has ordered the bank to raise five million in capital. However, having already reached the half-way mark of that goal, CEO Paul C. Hudson, has challenged his institution to raise double that amount.

“Just to have some cushion,” Hudson explains as the reason behind the ambitious goal. “You can do more if you have that kind of leeway where you’re not looking over your shoulder.”

At it’s inception, the fact that the black-owned bank catered to African-Americans who were being red-lined by traditional banks posed a threat to the lily white neighborhood it silently became a part of. Sure enough, in the years to come steady business boomed as “white flight,” the migration of white families from the city to the suburbs during the 50s and 60s, took hold of communities across the nation. Presently the banks are riding the more temperamental waves of the national economic crisis.

“The economy is such that everybody is making different decisions and we’re not immune to that,” explains Broadway Federal rep, Karen Hudson in an attempt to assure consumers that the bank is still in good financial condition. “We’re trying to make good business decisions that are profitable for everybody at the bank... We’re giving away just as much money in the community. I give away the money, I know this for a fact. I can’t say what’s going to happen next year or the year after but this year will not be affected.”

Bank on LA, a collaborative initiative focused on connecting Los Angeles’ unbanked and under-banked population which offers free financial education classes to help strengthen an individuals knowledge of money management and saving, stresses the importance of Broadway Fed’s presence.

“Many times those who are most in need of vital programs and services are limited in accessing them because the ‘brick and mortar’ buildings that house these valuable services are not in the community,” said Pat Clary, managing director of Bank on L.A. “Having the ability to open a checking or savings account in the neighborhood where you live is very important to the health and well-being of a community. The relationship that is established between the local bank or credit union and the customer, is many times the first step in a relationship that leads to the pathway of financial stability for individuals and their families.”

The upside of the bad news is that the bank is just closing branches, but not closing it’s operations as a whole. The Leimert Park branch, which has seen numerous closures of surrounding businesses, will close it’s doors on Oct. 22, after which, accounts will be transferred to the Inglewood branch on Oct. 24. The smallest branch on Wilshire, which shares space with the corporate office (which will remain open) serves it’s last day on Nov. 5 and all accounts will transfer to midtown on Nov. 7.

Across town, the original Broadway Federal office building at 4329 S. Broadway remains vacant. The dilapidated building crowds between an auto repair shop and a beauty salon, both of which, like all the rest of the nearby businesses, are labeled with Spanish — a clear sign of the changing tide of the neighborhood over the years. Two blocks down, a vacant lot sits where Broadway’s 1954 flagship headquarters once stood. Both relics represent where Broadway Federal has been but more importantly, where it’s going.

After seven years of operating out of a temporary headquarters at 45th and Broadway because of it’s commitment to serving the community which had suffered an estimated $1 billion in damages during the 1992 uprising, a state-of-the-art headquarters was opened on Figueroa and Martin Luther King Boulevard near USC in 1999 to replace the branch that burned down.

Broadway Fed is simply pruning the trees to make way for bigger and better things once the economy rebounds, Karen said.

In fact, Karen says in addition to securing more investors, the next item on the top of the bank's agenda is planning their 65th anniversary celebration this November with a huge surprise.
