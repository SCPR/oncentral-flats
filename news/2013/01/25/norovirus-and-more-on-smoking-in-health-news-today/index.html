Norovirus and more on smoking: In health news today

Jan. 25, 2013, 9:24 a.m.

Women and men officially have an equal mortality risk from smoking, says new research. (InqueGeek/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

Flu prevalence seems to be leveling off across the nation – except in the West. It's still rising there, according to the Associated Press, and the season's pediatric death toll is currently resting at 37.

Another type of flu – H1N1, commonly known as swine flu – affected one in five people worldwide during the first year of the 2009-10 pandemic, but the death rate stayed low. Reuters says a study that looked at data from 19 countries and some 90,000 blood samples found that the death rate that year was just 0.02 percent.

Not all the virus news is optimistic, though. U.S. News & World Report says a new strand of the norovirus stomach bug is spreading throughout the United States, and it's particularly risky for children and the elderly. Symptoms can last up to three days and include nausea, vomiting, diarrhea and abdominal pain – and it's super contagious.

In an article alarmingly titled "Antibiotic 'apocalypse' warning," the BBC quotes the chief medical officer of England saying that spike in drug-resistant infections is as bad as the problem of global warming, and that undergoing routine surgery could become deadly because of the risk of infection. She cited ineffective antibiotic use – that is, overprescription and overuse – as one of the reasons for the rise in resistance.

USA Today has a disturbing report on a form of abuse called "reproductive coercion" – where a male partner sabotages contraception before or during intercourse in order to make an unwilling female partner pregnant. Researchers aren't sure how common the practice is, but the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists urged health care providers to screen women for signs of this abuse.

Forbes has news on two new studies that had three major conclusions: Smoking is still a major problem when it comes to health and mortality, especially among low-income and minority folks; women and men are now equal in their risk of early death due to smoking (25 times greater than non-smokers); and it's never too late to quit and reap the benefits of doing so.

Staring at people who have a disability isn't necessarily due to bad manners, a new USC study suggests. KPCC reports that staring might, in fact, "be a way to understand and learn about them better."

A lab has succeeded in creating HIV-resistant cells, according to the San Francisco Chronicle, which, if it proves successful in human subjects, could have a profound impact on the way HIV-positive people are treated.

Talk about comfort food: HealthDay reports on a new study which showed people who were consistently exposed to bad news ate about 40 percent more food than people given "neutral messages." Researchers also found that tough times led people to crave high-calorie food. The solution? Tune out bad news as best you can, says the study.

How much do you think you weigh? If a new study is any indication, you're probably off. The Los Angeles Times says women and men, particularly those who are obese, have become increasingly likely to underestimate their actual weight. Researchers said it's possible that's at least partly due to denial.

And finally: Researchers don't exactly know how this works, but it seems doing small acts of kindness and expressing gratitude can make people happier. HealthDay adds the report hasn't been published in a peer-reviewed journal yet, and thus should be considered preliminary. But what's the harm in trying?
