Hospital visits and sex with a condom: In health news today

Jan. 23, 2013, 8:53 a.m.

Condoms don't appear to reduce the level of couples' sexual pleasure, according to a new study. (Simon Q/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

Almost one in five patients who are discharged from hospitals come back for acute care within a month, according to a new report. The Los Angeles Times says more than 57 percent of those visits were within the first 14 days after release.

Wired has news on a new brain scan that could alert football players of a condition called chronic traumatic encephalopathy – a "degenerative neurological disease that has been linked to repeated head trauma." You know, like the kind football players undergo on a daily basis. If the test is effective, that's huge for doctors, who will be able to find the condition before a player does irreparable damage to his brain. Previously, the condition could only be diagnosed posthumously.

A new study says daily physical education classes would increase kids' daily activity by about 23 minutes, which takes care of about one-third of federal exercise recommendations. HealthDay says the findings indicate cutting back on P.E. classes is a mistake that could take a significant toll on children's health.

Medicaid patients may soon be looking at higher fees if a proposed federal policy becomes law. The New York Times says the new rules would allow state Medicaid officials to charge higher co-pays and higher premiums for doctors' services, prescription drugs and some kinds of hospital care. In the proposed policy, the Obama Administration cited problems it had encountered in implementing health reform.

The New York state branch of the NAACP and the Hispanic Federation are protesting Mayor Michael Bloomberg's sugary drink-size limit, saying it puts minority-owned delis and corner stores at a competitive disadvantage to grocery chains. According to the Huffington Post, both organizations said they're concerned about "freedom of choice in low-income communities" in a lawsuit.

If you're worried about using a condom because you think it'll reduce your pleasure, don't be. That's according to a new study, says HealthDay, which found that a nationally representative sample of men and women said sex was overwhelmingly satisfying, whether or not a condom was used. Researchers also found that many people underestimate the effectiveness of lubricants in improving the sexual experience.

KPCC has news on a report which found that teenagers whose immediate family members are sent to war are more likely than their peers to use alcohol, drugs or tobacco. Drug and alcohol use seems to be highest among teens who had a sibling sent to war.

Young black women who live in urban settings and are exposed to severe abuse within their families are much more likely to fall victim to dating violence than their peers, according to a new study appearing in the journal Violence Against Women. Among 180 high school girls from a poor Chicago community, 85 percent had witnessed domestic abuse in their homes, and 72 percent were physically abused themselves.

Gay black men who want to come out to their family face a particularly unique set of challenges, says a new study in the Journal of GLBT Family Studies – not the least of which is "further hurting the image of African-American men." Researchers noted that parents might view a child's homosexuality as "one more strike against" him in a world that's already hard on black people.

HealthDay has more news on what too much sitting can mean for your health – and unsurprisingly, it's not good: Folks diagnosed with colorectal cancer who sit around a lot have a higher risk of dying.

And finally, also from HealthDay: A new study indicates that the happier you are in your marriage, the more likely you are to gain weight – together.
