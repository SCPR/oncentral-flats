COLUMN: Choosing to do business on Central Avenue

June 17, 2011, 9:54 a.m.

Photo by Grant Slater


By Lynda Wilson
I am a business owner located on Central Avenue
Comment

Tweet

As a native Los Angeleno, my family has resided in South Los Angeles for over 100 years, and we are excited to be opening a Box Brothers – Boxes, Packaging and Shipping at Rittenhouse Square, 3320 South Central Avenue with a grand opening tentatively set for August 2011. (Stay tuned)

We chose Central Avenue because it is and always has been an integral part of our lives. The church I was raised in (formerly Cedar Grove Missionary Baptist Church) is directly behind Rittenhouse. Newton Division Police Station was a former liquor store and a Foster’s Freeze; where I would sneak off from church and buy root beer floats. I attended my first funeral there — my uncle Way Way — short for Wyland in 1963.

We lived 43rd Place between Wall and San Pedro. My grandmother lived on Maple and 30th; a residence we still own today. I attended West Vernon Elementary School and remember when the Ralphs Market on Vernon and Figueroa first opened (now a Ross Department Store). On Vernon there was Clay’s Barber Shop, the shoe repair man, doctors and dentists’ offices, print shops and other ancillary businesses that lined the street. On Wall St., there was Kim’s Market where I could buy a Nestles Crunch for 5 cents. On San Pedro Street is where the first Church’s Chicken was located in the neighborhood and where I got my bag snatched by some big kid on Halloween. Bill’s Taco House was located on Santa Barbara Avenue and is where I had my first taco and none has compared since. Unfortunately, when new owners bought it and requested that I pay before served at a seated table Bill’s Taco House was no longer. I played in the lush green grass and among the giant palm trees in South Park and spent many a summer day on the grounds Wrigley Field.

Growing up in South Los Angeles, I was always surrounded by businesses and organizations run by people in the community who had more than just a monetary investment in its infrastructure. Here, I experienced “community.” I learned how it operates, why it operates and what has sustained its economic viability since the 1800’s no matter how it looks to those who have no clue about its history.

South Los Angeles, Central Avenue in particular, always was and still is a viable vibrant economic market place — and that is why we choose to add another economically viable business to The Avenue and stay in a community that has given us so much.

The Avenue’s economic viability has survived through the years because of family-owned businesses like Bower’s Cleaners who has been on Central Avenue for over 60 years. This is a demonstration of economic stability — still standing. 27th Street Bakery is another.

These businesses are generational and it takes more than just some letters printed on a piece of paper handed out by an institution of higher learning that deems one qualified to run a successful business. A savvy entrepreneur, such as myself, cannot rely solely on the advice of so-called experts especially when a great percentage of them have never created a business, much less run one. For example, John H. Johnson’s publishing empire (a family-owned business) was not built from a loan guaranteed by the SBA. He borrowed $500 against his mother’s furniture to publish the first issue of Ebony Magazine, and the rest is what — his — story! Now this is an example of savvy. It was 1942! During World War II! “Creative financing” is not a new concept.

Economic development also depends on a business-friendly environment demonstrated by tax subsidies to small businesses. Establishment of policies and programs undertaken to increase local economic development by governments and by private agencies that favor small business is good business.

Local economic development programs include a wide range of initiatives, from programs designed to improve the human capital of individual workers, to financial and in-kind subsidies to professional athletic teams (when we get another one), to enterprise zones, designed to lure particular businesses (family-owned). Abolishing programs like economic development centers, Governor Brown, undermines “business-friendly”. I encourage family-owned businesses to take advantage of EDC’s because it makes a huge difference.

Advocates for economic development are members of the Central Avenue Business Association (“CABA”). Members gather to create, inform and support business to business interaction. If you are a family-owned business and want to establish it on The Avenue, contact Johnny Andrade jandrade@coalitionrcd.org for information.
