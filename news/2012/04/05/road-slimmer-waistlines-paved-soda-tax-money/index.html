Road to slimmer waistlines: Paved with soda taxes?

April 5, 2012, 3:54 p.m.

A new survey has found that 57 percent of Californian voters would favor giving government officials the ability to tax soda and other sweetened drinks to fight childhood obesity. (Credit: Vox Efx/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

Taxes may be a crucial weapon in the fight against childhood obesity.

A Field Poll survey released on Wednesday shows that attitudes toward obesity have shifted since 2003. Forty-eight percent of California voters currently consider unhealthy eating habits or a lack of exercise to be the greatest risk facing California kids today. That's up from 35 percent in 2003.

Additionally, 73 percent of voters in the state believe that obesity prevention efforts should involve the greater community in addition to the kids and their families. That's against 22 percent who said it's a "personal issue for kids/family only."

The same voters seem to be willing to put their money where their mouth is. Sixty-two percent of those polled supported the idea of the state imposing a special fee on soda and soft drinks if the money were used to fight childhood obesity. Fifty-seven percent said they'd favor giving local governments authority to tax goods like alcohol, cigarettes, junk food or sweetened beverages via some sort of ballot measure.

Soda has been found to be a major factor in exacerbating or causing childhood obesity.

Kristine Madsen, a doctor at the UCSF Benioff Children's Hospital, told the San Francisco Chronicle the poll's findings were "huge."

"People are willing to put money behind it," she said. "That's one of the most important pieces to this entire [effort]." Although the poll itself didn't specify the monetary value of the hypothetical soda tax, Madsen told the Chronicle that a tax like that could be highly effective in reducing soda consumption.

"It's one way of tackling it," she added. "It's one more tool that we can use to try to help kids to the default behavior is the healthy behavior.

In January, the Los Angeles Times reported on one version of a soda tax that would charge a penny per ounce of sugar-sweetened beverage that would raise $13 billion a year and save $17 billion in medical expenses by reducing cases of heart disease and diabetes.

Forbes noted that particular tax would give a family of four with an average soda consumption a tax burden of $230.40 every year.

So a tax could add up pretty quickly. But for some advocates, that's the point – to get people to stop spending so much on soda. Those advocates are likely looking at numbers like the ones the L.A. County Department of Public Health put out in 2008 that said 29.5 percent of children in L.A. City Council District 9 were obese, a percentage that was night than most other areas in the county.

Councilwoman Jan Perry, who oversees that district, recently told OnCentral that childhood obesity was one of the "very basic challenges" her constituents still grapple with.

Poll participants also said that illegal drug use was the second greatest risk for California's youth, and new numbers suggest they might be right.

The poll was funded by the California Endowment, a supporter of OnCentral.
