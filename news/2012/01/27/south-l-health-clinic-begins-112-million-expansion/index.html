South L.A. health clinic begins $11.2-million expansion

Jan. 27, 2012, 10:04 a.m.

According to Jim Mangia, president and CEO of St. John's Well Child and Family Center, between 60 and 65 percent of the patients St. John's serves are uninsured, and about half of the residents in the entire area are without health insurance.


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

A South L.A. health clinic which currently serves over 40,000 patients every year is beginning an $11.2-million expansion, the Los Angeles Times reported Wednesday.

St. John's Well Child and Family Center, at the corner of Hoover and 58th, broke ground Wednesday on an $11.2 million expansion and renovation of two health centers in South Los Angeles. Once the project is over, St. John's will have 15 additional exam rooms, eight dental chairs and more space to provide health education, mental health care and insurance enrollment assistance. St. John's President and CEO Jim Mangia said this is crucial in what he called a "substantially medically underserved area that has very few physician offices, doctors and hospitals."

"The community is desperately in need of expanded primary care and specialty care services," he told OnCentral. "Most live below the federal poverty line, many are unemployed and more than half the residents in the community have no health insurance." St. John's provides free healthcare for all of its patients, and Mangia said the expansion was critical in order to keep up with need in the community.

And the need is great -- Mangia said between 60 and 65 percent of the patients St. John's serves are uninsured, and about half of the residents in the entire area are without health insurance. The goal of the expansion is to provide St. John's with the means to provide care to 8,968 more patients per year; the ratio of residents of primary care physicians in this area of South L.A. is 8,603:1 (compared to California's ratio of 190:1). Last year the clinic totaled 150,000 visits.

"Anyone is eligible" to receive healthcare at St. John's, said Mangia, "and we specifically target folks who are uninsured and have public health insurance, because those are the folks who have the least access to healthcare in L.A. and across the country."

Mangia, who referred to St. John's as a "safety net organization," said renovations are expected to be completed in a year and three months.
