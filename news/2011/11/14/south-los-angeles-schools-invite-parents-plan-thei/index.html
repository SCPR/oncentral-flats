Education

South Los Angeles schools ask parents for ideas

Nov. 14, 2011, 12:14 p.m.


By Rodrigo Mejia
Comment

Tweet

While rain fell onto much of Los Angeles over the weekend, parents and school officials filed into Johnnie Cochran Middle School on Saturday morning to discuss the future of four underperforming L.A. schools before they’re committed to restructuring.

The forum is the second in a series of four meetings where parents of Dorsey Senior High School, Los Angeles Senior High School, Cochran Middle School and 42nd Street Elementary School could offer thoughts and proposals to be submitted to LAUSD under their Public School Choice program.

Separate from other implementations of LASUD’s program, this time is the inclusion of parents. The goals is to end up with a transplanted system of schooling that won’t be rejected by the community.

“The common fear is that parents feel that someone is taking over their school when really, they don’t understand what’s happening,” said Robin Cole of Urban Issus Forum, which co-hosted the dialogue at the middle school. “The choice that they have as parents isn’t to have their school taken over, but to have the quality of education increased."

Representing the parents of 42nd Street Elementary School in South Los Angeles were two grandmothers, 70-year-old Billie Smith and 65-year-old Jacquelyn Smith Conkleton. They wove a narrative telling both of the academic shortcomings of the school and the hindrances each student endures.

“It’s hard for these children to go to school when they come with baggage,” said Conkleton, who is also a Special Education Representative for LAUSD.

“Their minds are congested with emotional problems,” she said, pointing out that it is partly responsible for the 54 percent of students missing seven or more days of school during the year. LAUSD as a whole has a 46 percent rate.

The performance of special education students at 42nd Street is also one of the many reasons warranting the school’s involvement in the program.

Since 2009, proficiency in English and math among African American students enrolled in special education was less than half the LAUSD average. Math is particularly disparate with only 4 percent of students testing for proficiency in the subject as compared to the 40 percent average among other LAUSD schools.

But schools also look to parents to shoulder the weight of trying to make changes and the topic held extra weight for the two people who attended on the grey and cold Saturday.

“A lot of parents don’t know about [this], about these meetings going on. A lot of input will make a difference. These parents don’t think it will make a difference but it will,” Conkleton said.

The third session will be held at Cochran Middle School on Saturday, December 10 from 8:30 am – Noon

The discussion will be centered around the details of the plan for each school in light of the conclusions made from the previous meetings, including parent’s input and other recommendations.
