SoLa Food Co-op works to offer healthy options in South Los Angeles

Sept. 28, 2011, 5:17 p.m.

Click to View Video


Video: Click below to play

By Jenna Bernard
Comment

Tweet

A yoga studio in Leimert Park is acting as a temporary office where a group of people are working with nothing more than a few laptops and a great idea, the SoLa Food Coop.

The SoLa Food Co-op is a start-up business in South Los Angeles. The founders of the project are planning to offer healthy food at an affordable price for the people isolated in an area with few options.

Bahni Turpin, 49, is a teacher at the yoga studio, but she is also the one pioneering the idea for a cooperative grocery store. Since August, Turpin has recruited more than 50 of her fellow yogis and neighbors around the community to make her dream a reality.

Turpin approached Milo Clow about the co-op at the weekly farmer's market in the Crenshaw Plaza.

“The experience has been great. Its been a wonderful way to network and find new, interesting connections within the neighborhood,” said Clow, who is now one of the leading members steering the growth of the business.

The farmer's market is the only place South L.A. residents can find organic and locally grown food in the area. A food cooperative would make those options available every day of the week.

What's the different? A food cooperative is a grocery store owned and managed by its members. There is a membership fee that can be paid in increments once a month or in full upfront. Anyone can shop at the co-op, but the intention is for the business to be self-sustaining and that is achieved through the financial assistance of the members.

Clow explained the fee pays itself back through the rewards program offered to members only.

“The more you spend at the local coop, the more you get out of it at the end of the year,” Clow said.

Just because South Los Angeles is a bit lacking when it comes to health food havens, there are still plenty of people campaigning for it.

Gwendolyn Flynn is the Policy Director of Community and Health Education for Community Health Councils, which is a nonprofit health policy advocate organization.

“To hear that there is a group of folks that are interested in establishing a food coop music to our ears,” said Flynn, encouraging support for a variety of food options in South LA.

Other health food advocates applaud the effort, but warn that it will be a difficult process.

“The start-up costs are significant and they'll need enough loyal customers willing to pay for food at a level that allows a grocery store to be open every day,” said Luis Sierra, the Assistant Director for the CA Center for Cooperative Development.

The SoLA food Coop is only in its beginning phases of their challenging two year start-up. Right now, the company is focusing on expanding its outreach. It is growing daily.

The co-op will sell certified organic, non-GMO, and locally grown products. Clow said that South L.A. residents find themselves driving to Hollywood, Santa Monica, or Long Beach to find healthy and natural food.

This makes South L.A. is the ideal place to start a food coop, Clow said. There is a large population to reach out to and there isn’t much competition.

“Santa Monica has a very successful food coop, but they are in competition with everything. Whole foods, Trader Joes, small independent markets,” Clow said.

Clow sad the co-op it offers employment opportunities, educational outreach, and networking for businesses in the area. The co-op is run by the community and for the community, which helps the local residents in more ways than health.

“You start to patronize those people’s businesses because you didn’t know,” Clow said.
