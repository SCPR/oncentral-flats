Off the streets and into uniform: The Newton Cadets

March 29, 2012, 11:06 p.m.

Cadets work out in the LAPD Newton Division parking lot after their general meeting. According to Newton officials, the program hosts the biggest membership of youth in the area. (Credit: Brian De Los Santos)


By Brian De Los Santos
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

It’s sort of a tradition in some of the families who live near Central Avenue in South Los Angeles.

Cynthia Romero, 17, enrolled in the LAPD Newton Cadet program after seeing her stepbrother’s involvement. She said she would see how they would train in the parking lot of the station and it sparked a sense of curiosity to join the Cadets.

That was three years ago - now she serves as a cadet sergeant.

“Once I started, I couldn’t let it go anymore,” Romero said. She added that the cadet program has been beneficial for her in many aspects.

“I’ve learned so many things besides self-discipline and how to be able to communicate with different people from different ages, different backgrounds,” she said.

The history

LAPD Officer Danny N. Chau, who has been one of the cadets’ coordinators since 2003, said the history of the cadet program has integrated into the Newton division and the neighborhood.

The Newton Cadet program has over 100 students and is one of the largest cadet groups in the region, according to Newton officials.

Romero said part of the reason being because the LAPD Newton division is located in South L.A. and parents push for their children to get involved and off the streets. Chau said that the program provides them an opportunity to “do a positive thing in the community.”

The community

Most often, word of mouth between neighbors and family members about past experiences in the cadet program drives people like David Tenche, who recently signed up his son, Micahel Tenche, to the academy. The family conveniently lives a couple of blocks away from the station.

“[Michael’s] brother graduated from [the program], so we wanted him to come,” Tenche said in Spanish.

He said some of the things they learn through the cadet academy are discipline and responsibility, which are qualities he wanted Michael to gain. Tenche said he is a big supporter of the cadets.

Chau said the retention for continued participation is high and there is a small percentage of kids that move on to be part of law enforcement.

The alumnus

Raul Macias, 21, is one of the cadets who aspires to be in law enforcement one day. “I already knew from that young age of 13 that I wanted to be a police officer,” Macias said.

He was involved with the cadets for seven years and stood the highest rank of a cadet sergeant until he had to turn in his uniform because the program only accommodates people until the age of 21. “I still want to have that chance to wear my uniform again, which I can’t because once you’re 21 you have to resign,” he said.

But he stays involved by visiting during training sessions and helps with mentoring the youth. “I miss it," he said. "That’s why I’m here all the time, trying to mentor these kids, trying to help them out, how to run stuff and be coherent."

As far as his social life is concerned, Macias and Romero both said teasing occurs from peers but there are also people who think working in the police is something to be proud of. But Romero said she does not let negative comments get to her.

“You end up being different even though you’re from the same neighborhood. You end up being able to see more than anybody else,” Romero said.

To be eligible for the program, you must be at least 13 years old, have a 1.5-2.0 grade point average, go through a background check, take part of a 16-week long academy on Saturdays, purchase the uniform and meet on Wednesdays.
