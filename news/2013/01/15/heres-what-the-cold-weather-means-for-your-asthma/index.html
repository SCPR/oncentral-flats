Here's what the cold weather means for your asthma

Jan. 15, 2013, 10:11 a.m.

Wearing a scarf can help folks with asthma breathe easy in the cold. (chicageek/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

Angelenos and Southern Californians aren't known for their resilience when it comes to cold weather.

(Note: In this part of the U.S., "cold weather" means anything below 60 degrees.)

To be fair, it has been uncharacteristically cool: Downtown Los Angeles dropped to 37 degrees in recent days. But while temperatures are expected to warm and could hit 70 degrees by Thursday, some legitimate health concerns accompany the frigid temperatures L.A. has been experiencing.

One of those is asthma, said Dr. Joseph Dizon, who heads the Department of Allergy and Immunology for Kaiser Permanente West Los Angeles.

"The cold air is a trigger for asthma," said Dizon. "Now on the flip side, when it's cold like this, people tend to stay indoors. If you stay indoors, you're perhaps exposed to people who are maybe sick."

In other words, it's hard to win when it's flu season.

Asthma is a respiratory condition that affects the bronchial tubes inside the lungs that help transport oxygen from the air you breathe to the blood vessels. With asthma, the muscles around those tubes tend to constrict, making the flow of air more difficult. The tube itself also becomes inflamed, getting progressively narrower.

Neither of those things is good for breathing, as anyone who's had an asthma attack can attest to.

"Inside the lungs, it tends to be moist," said Dizon. "We breathe in air through our nose, and our nose tends to humidify the air that we breathe in. Cold air really dries up the moisture inside the lungs."

That can lead to tightness in the chest and coughing, added the doctor.

But folks with asthma don't have to avoid the cold entirely, said Dizon. Breathing through a scarf helps promotes moisture in the lungs, which alleviates breathing difficulty.

As for asthma patients who enjoy exercising outdoors, he recommends avoiding working out when the sun isn't out to combat the cold. He also said they should take their quick-release medication about 20 minutes before heading outside.

The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention have more on asthma.
