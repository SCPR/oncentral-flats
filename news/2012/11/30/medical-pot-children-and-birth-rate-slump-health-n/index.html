Medical pot for children and a birth rate slump; in health news today

Nov. 30, 2012, 11 a.m.

Birth rates are at an all-time low in the U.S. (Jake Johnson/Flickr Creative Commons)


By Hayley Fox
Comment

Tweet

Is medical marijuana safe for children? Debate continues on the issue after ABC reported on a 7-year-old in Oregon who was diagnosed with leukemia in July and has been taking cannabis oil pills to treat the disease. Although the child's mother credits the drug for forcing the cancer into remission, most doctors say pot does not fight cancer and in fact, may only decrease the child's immune system. It can however help ease pain and increase appetite, so it's often used to make people more comfortable while they are using other treatments for cancer.

CBS reports that America's birth rate is the lowest its been since 1920, according to a report from the Pew Research Center. The U.S. birth rate decreased by 8 percent between 2007 and 2010, and is now at about 63.2 births per 1,000 women who are of "childbearing age." Researchers attribute the falling birth rate mostly to the recession. In 2011 there were an estimated 3.95 million births, down from 4 million the previous year.

World AIDS day is Dec. 1 and it serves as an opportunity for people to show support for those living with HIV and honor those who have died from AIDS. More than 1.1 million people in the U.S. have HIV and almost 1 in 5 are unaware of their infection, according to the CDC. HIV can be transmitted when two people have unprotected sex, share needles or syringes, or when an infected mother gives birth. In 2011, 73 percent of new HIV infections in South Los Angeles were due to male-to-male sexual contact.

The Ronald Reagan UCLA Medical Center received a failing grade in patient safety on a new national report card, the Los Angeles Times reports. Issued by a non-profit, the medical center got an "F" overall in categories such as "preventing medical errors, patient infections and deaths." UCLA officials said the grade is not fair or accurate and is merely a result of the evaluating agency changing their grading style.

And finally...some good news! Yahoo news reports that according to a study out of Cal State Los Angeles, children in the U.S. are getting enough sleep based on recommendations from the CDC. Health officials say toddlers should be getting 12 to 14 hours of sleep, preschoolers should get 11 to 13 hours of sleep, and adolescents age 10 to 17 should get 8.5 to 9.5 hours of shut-eye. Research shows that there's no significant difference in the amount of sleep boys and girls are getting, and only notice a slight variation between Latino kids and white kids -- with Latinos getting on average 19 more minutes of sleep.
