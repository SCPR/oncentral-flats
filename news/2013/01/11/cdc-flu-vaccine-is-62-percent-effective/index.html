CDC: Flu vaccine is 62 percent effective

Jan. 11, 2013, 10:57 a.m.

Health officials estimate that this season's flu vaccine has been 62 percent effective. (UIC Pharmacy/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

In the midst a flu season that's been one of the worst in years, federal health officials said this year's flu vaccine is only moderately effective.

While estimates will likely change slightly by the end of flu season, what that means right now is that the vaccine has an effectiveness rate of 62 percent, which is in keeping with federal health experts' estimates based on earlier trials.

The data was published as part of the Jan. 11 edition of the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention's Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report.

The CDC wrote that "these early [vaccine effectiveness] estimates underscore that some vaccinated persons will become infected with influenza; there, antiviral medications should be used as recommended for treatment in patients, regardless of vaccination status."

Antivirals are a type of medication specifically intended to treat viral infections (like the flu), but instead of destroying the virus they slow down its development.

Despite the chance the vaccine will fail, Los Angeles County's top health official still says there's time to get it, and that it's in people's best interest to do so. Dr. Jonathan Fielding, the director of the county's Department of Public Health, said there's still plenty of vaccine.

"It takes 10 days to two weeks for your immunity to develop after the shot and, while the vaccine isn't perfect, it's the best way we have to prevent you from getting the flu or prevent you from having a very severe case," he told KPCC.

Both Fielding and the CDC said the season is expected to last at least a few more weeks.

Around one-third of people tested by CDC were positive for either influenza A or B during the week of Dec. 30 through Jan. 5. So far, 20 children have died a flu-related death this season.

While most states are reporting widespread flu activity, California seems to have been spared the brunt of the flu outbreak – at least, so far. Fielding said L.A. County is experiencing "roughly an average flu season," since there's more flu now than there was last year, but less than there was in 2010.

But it's still early – and even after the season peaks, there'll be weeks to go.
