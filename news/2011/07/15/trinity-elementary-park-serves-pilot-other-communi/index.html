Trinity Elementary park serves as pilot for other community parks

July 15, 2011, 1:28 p.m.


By Dion Rabouin
Comment

Tweet

Trinity Elementary School recently became the pilot for a “greening” development set to happen in eight South Los Angeles schools. The project will implant gardens, trees and grass at schools currently in short supply of plant life.

After researching and writing the “Community School Parks Report” in 2008, Los Angeles Unified School District (LAUSD) found that in comparison to other major US cities, Los Angeles was lacking in parks, especially in South LA. The project at Trinity was a collaboration between the district and organizations like People for Parks, EnrichLA and Tree People.

“[Trinity was] identified as a target school that would be really, truly an amazing site to be used as a community park,” said Michelle Bagnat, Urban Forestry Manager for Tree People. “They looked at the area and said, ‘My gosh, there are no parks, where are the kids going in the summer? Where are they going after school? So, all these organizations came together and said what can we do?’”

The trees will be installed and maintained by Tree People’s community green teams: teachers, students, neighbors and other members of the community. Tree People stays involved in the project for up to five years, with the goal of keeping the community and the school involved with one another.

There is another park, Trinity Park, is about one mile north of the school, but it's a small park and some in the area feel it isn't a safe environment for kids.

The park at Trinity Elementary is currently in the early stages of construction, but is expected to be complete by fall 2011. Once the asphalt has been removed and the greens have been installed, the “pocket park” will serve as an on-campus recreation and learning area for students during school hours and will be open to the public afterward.

Trinity already has some elements of the greening process to show. Before the 2010-11 school year ended, the all-volunteer organization EnrichLA created and installed an edible garden on the grounds. Tomas O’Grady, founder and director of EnrichLA, said the garden can and will serve many functions for the school and the neighborhood.

“A garden has a learning component, it has a beauty component and it has a calming component,” O’Grady said. “And let’s be honest, you as a human being are going to be more calm in a garden than you would be on a highway.”

O’Grady began installing gardens in schools two years ago at Thomas Starr King Middle School. He said it was at King that he realized the good a garden could do.

“We saw the effect that it had on the school, both in getting community members to come in and volunteer and cheering up the school campus and giving teachers a little spring in their step,” said O’Grady. “It’s tangible and it’s real. It’s right there. The garden became a way for the school to show off to the community, so that the school could have a better image.”

While EnrichLA was working to plant gardens, groups like Tree People and People for Parks are planning to come into the same schools and replace the concrete with dirt, grass and trees. In addition to the garden and park at Trinity, EnrichLA is also working on projects at John Adams Middle School, Main Street School and Vernon Elementary. Tree People is also considering adding trees to those schools and possibly five others.

So far, eight elementary schools — Calvert, Beethoven, Trinity, Vine, Carthay Center, Dorris Place, Saturn and Yorkdale — have received joint-use bond funds from LAUSD for greening projects. The district plans to examine the success of the pilot project at Trinity and may continue the effort throughout the city. In addition to improving the school’s aesthetic, LAUSD is hoping these projects will help out in other ways.

“We hope to increase parental and community involvement,” said LAUSD spokeswoman Ana Lasso. “Also, these projects will be a great opportunity for instructors to integrate math, language arts and science standards in outdoor learning settings.”

Lasso said the parks could also serve as a way to get LA schoolchildren to be more active and learn about nutrition.

The construction of pocket parks and gardens at schools in South LA is expected to continue through at least 2012. Although, this effort is going to be ending anytime soon.

“We’re finding that by being on different committees and hearing what everybody wants to do there’s a thread that’s being woven throughout,” Bagnat said. “There’s a common thread and there’s a lot of people who have a deep interest right now in South Los Angeles.”

Work at Trinity will be ongoing throughout the summer. There is not yet an official start date for parks at any other schools.
