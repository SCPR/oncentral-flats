Calif. gets an F for tobacco prevention spending; cigarette tax given a D

Jan. 16, 2013, 10:52 a.m.

Cigars have become more popular, according to a new report from the American Lung Association. (seagers/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

A scathing report from the American Lung Association (ALA) blasted federal and state governments for failing to adequately regulate tobacco in their respective jurisdictions.

The "State of Tobacco Control 2013" report grades the federal government, Washington, D.C. and all 50 state governments on their tobacco laws, and whether they're "protecting citizens from the enormous toll tobacco use takes on lives and the economy."

The short answer, according to ALA: They're not.

The report says despite the fact that smoking costs the U.S. about $200 billion in health care and lost productivity each year, the federal government hasn't pursued the industry aggressively enough – even though it launched its first national ad campaign, which ran 12 weeks and cost about $54 million in 2012. As far as the states, the report's authors claim that their governments "continue to look the other way" and haven't invested billions gleaned from tobacco taxes and settlements that "should be directed to effectively prevent kids from starting to use tobacco and help current tobacco users quit."

In short, ALA characterizess the federal government's actions regarding tobacco as "missed opportunities to save lives." The Food and Drug Administration, it says, has been "largely absent" when it comes to regulating tobacco products, and "inactive" on implementing recommendations from its Tobacco Products Scientific Advisory Committee. All of that plus stalled legislation that would theoretically help smokers quit – if it were passed – makes for a pretty lackluster federal performance in ALA's eyes.

Meanwhile, says the report, the tobacco industry continues to grow more powerful, with significant market shifts taking place. (Example: Cigars are becoming more popular.) ALA claims that's in part because state governments are "awash in tobacco money" but "don't spend them on reducing tobacco use."

California is no exception: It wasgiven an F for tobacco prevention control and spending, as well as its effort to help Golden State smokers quit. Among the report's findings:

– The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) recommends that for the 2012-13 fiscal year, California spends around $442 million on tobacco control programs. At last check, the state had around $68.6 million slated for this cause, meaning it's spending a less-than-solid 15.5 percent of the CDC's recommendation.

– Medi-Cal, the state's Medicaid program, covers the cost of a fair number of tobacco cessation methods (the patch or gum, for example).

– The CDC recommends that California invests $10.53 per smoker on its quitline (1-800-QUIT-NOW); the state invests $2.40.

– California law doesn't require private insurance to cover smoking cessation.

– California's cigarette tax didn't quite get an F: At $0.87 per pack of 20, ALA gave the state a D.

There was some good news for California: It got an A for its smoke-free air – and the strong regulations that try to keep it that way. That means that tobacco use is prohibited in government workplaces and public schools, and restricted in private work environments, restaurants and bars, to name a few examples.

Still, noted ALA, 3.6 million Californians smoke, and it cost the state more than $18.1 billion in 2011. That same year, more than 36,600 deaths could be attributed to smoking, and another 21,500-plus were attributable to smoking-relating diseases.

A recent report from the state's Department of Public Health named illegal sales to minors as California's biggest tobacco problem.
