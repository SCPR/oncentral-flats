Flu still going strong and armpit genetics: In health news today

Jan. 18, 2013, 9:28 a.m.

Deodorant on a store shelf. Two percent of people in a study carried a gene that prevented their armpits from producing an offensive odor. (Neil Conway/Flickr Creative Commons)


By José Martinez
OnCentral
Comment

Tweet

Flu activity is still going strong but waning in some areas, reports USA Today. The death toll of this year's virus is above epidemic levels: So far, at least 29 children have died.

The good news from the American Cancer Society's latest report: The disease's prevalence rate in the U.S. has been on a major decline since 1991. The awful news, according to the Los Angeles Times: 2013 will see more than 1.66 million new cases and more than 580,000 cancer deaths.

California's in trouble, according to a new report focusing on dental care in the state. KPCC reports that the state is preparing to transition almost a million more young people to Medi-Cal under the Affordable Care Act, where the dental programs are already overburdened.

How expensive is health care? HealthDay reports on a new study which found that in order to make ends meet, people with high medical costs used credit cards, sold their cars and even started dodging debt collectors. Perhaps even worse: Some stopped going to treatment or taking their medicine.

Researchers have been able to identify people who anonymously donated their DNA to medical research, raising questions about whether privacy in that realm is something that can be promised or guaranteed. The BBC says the identification was possible because of large genetic databases that help people trace their family trees.

There can't be much difference between a family dinner that lasts 17 minutes and one that lasts 20 minutes, right? Wrong, according to new research appearing in the journal Economics and Human Biology, which says low-income families who make an effort to engage their children for a full 20-minute meal at least four times a week help their children keep a healthy weight. While a 15- or 17-minute meal may seem like basically the same thing, researchers say those extra minutes add up.

HealthDay says minority, HIV-positive patients who work with a "culturally-competent" doctor receive better care. In this case, cultural competence boils down to "a combination of awareness, attitudes, skills and behaviors" that impact a health provider's ability to care for a diversity of patients.

Men who need a reason to avoid drugs, take heed of a study appearing in the Journal of Sexual Medicine: Substance abuse seems to negatively affect men's sexual performance – even if they've been clean for years.

All this talk about guns doesn't seem to be doing much to curb ownership. In fact, reports HealthDay, a new study shows that many young Americans – 40 percent of those surveyed – plan to own guns at a later point, which could indicate the reversal of a trend of decreasing gun ownership.

And finally: Are you part of the 2 percent? Smithsonian.com says 2 percent of participants in a new study carried a rare version of a gene that prevents their armpits from becoming smelly. Still, 78 percent of those lucky people wear deodorant on all or most days.
